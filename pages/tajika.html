Tājika varṣaphala — the Indo-Persian annual chart

The varṣaphala ("fruit of the year") is Jyotiṣa's annual-chart system — but its grammar is Arabic, carried into Sanskrit by the 13th-century Tājika school: itthaśāla and īsarāpha are ittiṣāl and inṣirāf, the very applying and separating that Lilly's horary uses. This tool computes the varṣa-praveśa (the sidereal solar return), the munthā, the varṣeśvara (ruler of the year, from the five candidates), the Tājika aspects with their deeptāṁśa orbs, and the core dozen sahams — every rule cited to Balabhadra's Hāyanaratna (1649) in Martin Gansten's critical edition (Brill 2020), with the tradition's own disagreements flagged, never silently resolved.

Honest note — please read This is a historical calculation grammar, reproduced faithfully for study. The astronomy underneath (the return instant, the positions) is real and verifiable; the idea that the year's ruler or a saham "means" anything has no demonstrated predictive validity — astrology is regarded by the scientific community as a pseudoscience. Where the tradition disagrees with itself (the +30° saham correction that Viśvanātha rejected, the varṣeśvara fallback that four authorities settle four ways, the Mitra phrasing, Gaurava's night form, Putra's reversal), the fork is shown in the data. How this works — the varṣaphala, step by step

The varṣa-praveśa is sidereal

The annual chart is cast for the instant the Sun "attains the same sign, degree, minute and second" as at birth — measured in the sidereal zodiac (the Hāyanaratna reckons with the Sūrya-siddhānta sidereal year of 365.25875 days; Hāyanaratna ch.1 §6). This tool reuses the site's solar-return bisection (solar-return.js) but re-targets it: the return is found where the tropical Sun reaches natal sidereal longitude + ayanāṁśa(return instant), iterated to convergence. The sidereal frame uses the Lahiri ayanāṁśa — a modern convention, flagged as such: Balabhadra's own constants are Sūrya-siddhānta era. The sidereal return lands ≈20.4 minutes later than the tropical return per year of age.

The munthā

"Dividing the total years elapsed from the nativity by twelve, taking the remainder and counting from the ascendant, [the sign] where it finds rest will be the munthahā" (Samarasiṃha, quoted in Hāyanaratna ch.5 §1). Operationally: munthā sign = (natal ascendant sign + completed years) mod 12 — at age 0 it IS the natal ascendant sign. Within the year it progresses about 2.5° a month (strictly, the house extent ÷ 12 — a rule of the Tājikamuktāvali as quoted by Balabhadra). Its house in the annual chart sets the year's focus (ch.5 §2).

The varṣeśvara — five candidates, one precondition, one honest dispute

The ruler of the year is chosen from exactly five office-holders (pañcādhikārin): the munthā lord, the natal-lagna lord, the annual-lagna lord, the Tājika triplicity lord of the annual lagna (the system's own day/night table — day Sun/Venus/Saturn/Venus, night Jupiter/Moon/Mercury/Mars for fire/earth/air/water — not the sign ruler), and the lord of the Sun's sign by day / Moon's sign by night. The precondition is the aspect, not the muscle: "even [a planet] without strength, aspecting the ascendant, is ruler of the year; one not aspecting [the ascendant], although strong, is not" (Yādava). Ties run down the Tājikakaustubha chain: greatest aspect → greater strength → more claims among the five dignities (documented here, not computed) → the munthā lord. When no candidate aspects the lagna, the authorities genuinely split four ways (Yādava: munthā lord · Tuka: annual-lagna lord · Gaṇeśa: the candidate that aspected in the nativity · Balabhadra: strongest in ṣaḍbala, ties to the munthā lord); this tool implements Balabhadra's verdict and displays the dispute whenever the fallback fires.

Tājika aspects, deeptāṁśa orbs, and the Arabic bridge

Aspects are counted sign to sign: 5th/9th open friendship (¾ strength), 3rd/11th secret friendship (¼), 4th/10th secret enmity (½), 7th open enmity (full), and the conjunction a full aspect "similar to the mutual aspect on the seventh" (Hāyanaratna ch.2, with Vāmana's strengths). A configuration operates within the planets' deeptāṁśa ("orbs of light"): Sun 15°, Moon 12°, Mars 8°, Mercury 7°, Jupiter 9°, Venus 7°, Saturn 9° (Daivajñālaṃkṛti 8.9 = Tājikasāra 88; the Praśnavaiṣṇava alone adds Rāhu 12°, and one popular website's "Mars 12°" is a transcription error). The text's own combining rule is "within their own orbs of light, or within twelve degrees"; the modern half-sum convention is unverified in the sources — though it is mathematically identical to this site's Lilly moiety rule. Itthaśāla (ittiṣāl) is the applying configuration: the swifter planet behind the slower within orb; īsarāpha (inṣirāf) is separation — but only once the swifter planet is a full degree past exactness (Tājikabhūṣaṇa 4.10): past by less than 1° is still itthaśāla, "so says the ancient commentator" (Hāyanaratna ch.3 §4). That sliver diverges from the Western applying flag of this site's aspects.js, and the tables mark it. Nakta, yamayā and kambūla are the translation, collection and reception of light — the same doctrine Lilly learned from the same Arabic sources (Gansten 2020 for the transmission).

The sahams and the contested +30°

Every saham has the form minuend − subtrahend + added point (usually the Lagna; Venus for Mitra; Sun by day / Moon by night for Gaurava), the minuend and subtrahend swapping at night unless the text says otherwise (Bhrātṛ and Putra do not reverse; Gaurava has an explicit night form). Then the check: "if the ascendant is not between the signs of the subtrahend and the minuend … one sign should be added" — +30° when the added point falls outside the arc from subtrahend forward to minuend. The correction is general in Balabhadra and Raman — but Viśvanātha Daivajña's Prakāśikā (1629) rejects it outright ("no statement in support of this notion is found anywhere, nor is there any adding of one [sign] in the school of the Yavanas"). It is contested within the tradition; this tool applies it as the operational default and marks every saham where it fired.
