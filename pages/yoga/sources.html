The editions & the method

Every sūtra in this wing is built from public-domain scholarship, cited per record. This page lists the edition stack, states which source does what (with one correction to a common assumption), gives the pointers to the free scans, and sets out the transcription hygiene and the coverage this wing verified.

Note on dates: wisdomlib's reprint of Rāma Prasāda is dated 1924; the original Pāṇini Office printing is 1912, which is the date cited in-record. The identification of the bhāṣya author as "Vyāsa" is the traditional label — Maas (2013) argues the sūtras and bhāṣya are one work by one author; the wing keeps both, see the index.

Read the originals (free scans)

  • Woods (1914), HOS 17 — full text on the Internet Archive (item yogasystemofpata00wooduoft). Use the body text, not the Analytical Summary: some sūtra-only mirrors reproduce Woods's summary form, which can truncate a sūtra (e.g. its II.29 drops "are the eight aids").
  • Rāma Prasāda (1912), SBH IV — on the Internet Archive; each sūtra's gloss also mirrored per-page on wisdomlib.org. Transcribe from the page scans, not the DjVu OCR (verified OCR corruptions such as "Vjritti", "Nirodhalj").
  • Vivekananda (1896) — Raja Yoga in the Complete Works, vol. 1, on Wikisource.
  • Devanāgarī — sanskritdocuments.org, doc_yoga/yogasuutra.html (with and without Vedic accents; the wing uses the text with accents removed).
  • Bhoja's Rājamārtaṇḍa — Rājendralāla Mitra's edition, per-sūtra on wisdomlib; used to derive the Book-IV Bhoja numbering and the IV.34 genitive variant.

Transcription hygiene

  • Accents stripped. The sanskritdocuments file prints Vedic svara accents (udātta U+0951, anudātta U+0952). Every such mark is removed — the Yoga Sūtras are not an accented Vedic text — while avagraha (ऽ), anusvāra, visarga and virāma are preserved. The data modules assert no accent characters remain.
  • Woods body text, not the summary. Translations are taken from the HOS 17 running body text; a few of Woods's archaic transliterations were modernised (Īçvara → Īśvara), flagged in-record as "after Woods (1914)".
  • Glosses mapped by content, not by number. Rāma Prasāda's Book III has only 54 numbered sūtras (he folds the vulgate III.20 into his III.19), so his numbers run behind the vulgate; each gloss here is attached to the sūtra it actually belongs to, not to his number.
  • Two reading-variants flagged in-record rather than resolved: I.14 (satkārāsevito vs the satkāra-ādara-āsevito of Iyengar-lineage texts) and IV.34 (citiśaktir iti, nominative, in sanskritdocuments / Rāma Prasāda vs citiśakter iti, genitive, in Bhoja / Mitra).

Coverage & limits

The wing stores 196 records — the full inclusive text (Book I 51, Book II 55, Book III 56 with the variant, Book IV 34) — with dual numbering on Book III and a Bhoja number on Book IV, so the 194 / 195 / 196 totals are all derivable and none is silently privileged. The counts on the index are computed live from the data, not hard-typed.

The anchor sūtras (I.1–3, I.14, II.1, II.29–32, II.46–48, III.1–8, III.37, IV.34) and roughly a quarter of the remainder were spot-verified against Woods (1914) and, for the Devanāgarī, against sanskritdocuments and wisdomlib. Where a verified rendering corrects a common gloss it is noted on the theory page (the āsana list in particular: svastika = "the mystic-diagram", paryaṅka = "the bedstead", and the sthiraprasrabdhi reading belongs to Maas's critical edition, not Woods). The one honestly open item is the 11 / 12 / 13 count of the bhāṣya āsana list, which the sources themselves leave unresolved — so the wing leaves it open too.

The rule, again This is a historical philosophy and practice text, presented for study with citations throughout. Its claims — the postures, the breath-work, and above all the siddhis — are described here, never prescribed, and have no demonstrated validity as claims about the world. See Sources & Science.
