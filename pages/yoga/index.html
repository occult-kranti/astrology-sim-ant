The Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali — the study wing

The Pātañjala Yoga-sūtra is a compact treatise of aphorisms — 196 of them in the inclusive count — that defines yoga in its second line as citta-vṛtti-nirodha, "the restriction of the fluctuations of mind-stuff", and then lays out a path to that stilling: the five kleśas to be thinned, the eight limbs (aṣṭāṅga) to be practised, the catalogue of powers (vibhūti) that arise on the way — and the warning that those powers are obstacles, not the goal. This wing renders every sūtra word-by-word from public-domain scholarship — each with a gist of the classical Vyāsa-bhāṣya (after Woods 1914) collapsible beneath its translation — and studies the text honestly: what it says, where its editions disagree, and where later tradition put words in its mouth.

Honest note — please read This is a wing over a historical philosophy and practice text, presented for study. The Yoga Sūtras describe a contemplative discipline and a metaphysics (Sāṃkhya-derived: puruṣa and prakṛti, the guṇas, kaivalya); Book III catalogues siddhis — supernormal "powers" said to follow from saṃyama (knowledge of past and future, of others' minds, invisibility, levitation, and the rest). Those claims are the text's own, described here never prescribed. Patañjali himself calls the powers "obstacles to samādhi" (III.37) — a counterweight this wing keeps in view. Nothing here has demonstrated validity as a claim about the world; it is the history of an idea, read closely.

What the Yoga Sūtras are — and aren't

The text is traditionally attributed to Patañjali and comes bundled with a prose commentary, the Yoga-bhāṣya, traditionally ascribed to "Vyāsa". Modern scholarship revises both points, and this wing flags rather than resolves them:

  • Date. Philipp Maas dates the work to c. 325–425 CE (a working figure of ~400 CE), from synchronisms with the Buddhist philosopher Vasubandhu. This is the current leading estimate — presented as an estimate, not settled fact.
  • One author or two? Maas (2013) argues the sūtras and the "Vyāsa"-bhāṣya are one unified work (the Pātañjalayogaśāstra) by a single author-compiler, the bhāṣya being his own self-commentary — the separate "Vyāsa" a later tradition. This is the dominant view, but with dissent; the wing keeps the traditional "Vyāsa-bhāṣya" label alongside it.
  • Not the grammarian. The identification of this Patañjali with Patañjali the grammarian (of the Mahābhāṣya) is a post-Bhoja (11th-century) conflation, rejected by most modern scholars on linguistic grounds. Flagged, never resolved.

How many sūtras? The editions disagree

There is no single canonical sūtra count. The three living traditions differ over exactly two sūtras — the disputed III.22 and the vulgate IV.16 — giving totals of 194, 195 or 196.
