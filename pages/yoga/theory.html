The eight limbs, the āsana honesty layer & the powers

This page reads the architecture of the text: the eight limbs (aṣṭāṅga) declared in II.29 and where each is defined; the yamas and niyamas; the āsana honesty layer — the sūtras name no postures at all, and the familiar posture-names come from the commentary; the catalogue of powers (vibhūti) with the text's own warning attached; and kaivalya, the goal beyond every power.

The honest frame, in one line Everything below is the claim of a historical text and its commentaries, described never prescribed — the postures, the breath-work and above all the siddhis (powers) are what the tradition says, presented for study, with no demonstrated validity as a claim about the world.

The eight limbs (aṣṭāṅga) — the map of II.29

II.29 lists the eight aṅga — "limbs" or "aids", not a strict ladder — that carry the practitioner toward discriminative discernment. The last three, practised together on one object, become saṃyama (III.4); III.7 calls the inner three "direct aids", yet III.8 calls even them "outer" relative to seedless samādhi — the text relativises its own scheme.

The yamas & niyamas

The five yamas (II.30), kept universally — unconditioned by birth, place, time or circumstance — are the mahāvrata, the "great vow" (II.31):

  • ahiṃsā — non-harming
  • satya — truthfulness
  • asteya — non-stealing
  • brahmacarya — continence
  • aparigraha — non-possessiveness / non-acceptance of gifts

The five niyamas (II.32) — the last three of which are also the kriyā-yoga triad of II.1:

  • śauca — purity
  • santoṣa — contentment
  • tapas — austerity / self-castigation
  • svādhyāya — self-study / recitation
  • īśvara-praṇidhāna — devotion to the Īśvara

The āsana honesty layer

This is the point where the modern popular image of "yoga" and the actual text diverge most sharply, so the wing states it plainly. In the Yoga Sūtras, āsana gets exactly three sūtras and names no posture whatever:

  • II.46 — sthira-sukham āsanam, "stable-and-easy posture" (Woods).
  • II.47 — it is steadied by relaxing effort and by samāpatti on the infinite.
  • II.48 — thereafter one is unassailed by the pairs of opposites.

That is the whole doctrine of posture in the sūtras: a seat steady and comfortable enough to hold for contemplation. The familiar named postures appear one layer out, in the Vyāsa-bhāṣya on II.46 — and even there they are only named, not described. Descriptions come a further layer out still, from Vācaspatimiśra's Tattvavaiśāradī (9th–10th century CE) and the later haṭha-yoga texts.

A count that the sources leave open — flagged, not resolved The bhāṣya on II.46 names eleven postures, then adds two abstract terms (a "steady/relaxed" state and "whatever is comfortable") and closes open-endedly — ity-evam-ādīni, "and so on". Secondary sources therefore count the list as 11, 12 or 13. This wing does not pick a number.

The postures shown only as text notes

These are named in the bhāṣya but have no description that honestly supports a specific human figure. Inventing one would be fabrication, so they stay as notes:

  • krauñca-niṣadana ("the seated curlew"), hasti-niṣadana ("the seated elephant") and uṣṭra-niṣadana ("the seated camel") — Vācaspati says only that these "may be understood by actually seeing" the curlew, elephant and camel seated. They are animal analogies, not described shapes; no human-figure diagram is drawn for them.
  • sopāśraya ("with a support / with the rest") — a seated posture using a prop, but the two public-domain translations of Vācaspati disagree on what the prop is: Woods's Vācaspati has a yogic table or strap (yoga-paṭṭaka), while Rāma Prasāda's has "a tiger's skin or the deer skin or some cloth" to sit upon. sources diverge The prop is flagged, not resolved.
  • paryaṅka ("the bedstead") — Woods: "lying down with the arms stretched around the knees"; the commentary is ambiguous, so it is kept as a note.
  • sama-saṃsthāna ("the even arrangement") — the feet contracted and pressed together at the heels and tips; a marginal, schematic description only.
Mudrās are not in this text The mudrās (the "seals" of haṭha practice — mahāmudrā, khecarī, and the rest) appear nowhere in the Yoga Sūtras or the Vyāsa-bhāṣya. They belong to the later haṭha-yoga corpus (the Haṭhapradīpikā lists ten; the Gheraṇḍasaṃhitā twenty-five). If you meet them attached to "Patañjali's yoga", that is a later tradition read back onto the text.

The powers (vibhūti) — and the text's own counterweight

Book III catalogues the siddhis that saṃyama is said to yield — a list the modern reader meets with understandable scepticism, and which the wing presents as exactly what it is: the claims of the text. Among them: knowledge of past and future (III.16), of the cries of all beings (III.17), of former births (III.18) and of others' minds (III.19); invisibility (III.21); foreknowledge of death (III.23); knowledge of the cosmos from the sun, the moon and the pole-star (III.27–29); entering another's body (III.39); levitation and passage through space (III.43); mastery of the elements and the senses (III.45–49); and finally omniscience (III.50).

III.37 — the pāda judges its own powers

te samādhāv upasargā vyutthāne siddhayaḥ — "In concentration these [powers] are obstacles; in the emergent state they are perfections" (III.37, vulgate). The text frames its whole catalogue of siddhis as an impediment to the higher goal: they are "perfections" only from the standpoint of the outgoing, distracted mind. III.51 makes the same point — dispassion even toward omniscience is what opens onto kaivalya; and III.52 warns the yogin to feel neither attachment nor pride when "those in high places" come inviting.

This is the counterweight the wing keeps in view: the Yoga Sūtras themselves treat the powers as a temptation to be renounced, not a prize to be sought — which is precisely why they can be studied here as the history of an idea, described never prescribed.

Kaivalya — the goal beyond every power

Kaivalya ("isolation", "aloneness", absolute independence) is the summit of the whole system, and both Book III and Book IV end on it. In the Sāṃkhya-derived metaphysics of the text, bondage is the conjunction of the seer (puruṣa, pure consciousness) with the seen (prakṛti and its three guṇas); liberation is their separation, brought about by unwavering discriminative discernment (viveka-khyāti). The closing sūtra, IV.34, defines it twice over: kaivalya is the inverse resolution of the guṇas — matter, its purpose for the Self now spent, returning to its source — "or it is the Energy of Intellect (citi-śakti) grounded in its own form". With that iti the treatise closes.

Book II reaches the same word by a different road: II.25 defines kaivalya as the cessation of the seer–seen correlation once avidyā is gone. Book IV adds that with the dharma-megha ("rain-cloud of dharma") concentration the afflictions and karma are uprooted and the sage is released "even while yet alive" (IV.30).

How this study wing renders the sūtras

Each sūtra record is assembled from three public-domain layers, cited per record:

  • Devanāgarī from sanskritdocuments.org, with the Vedic pitch-accents that edition carries (udātta U+0951, anudātta U+0952) stripped — the Yoga Sūtras are not an accented Vedic text.
  • Word-by-word glosses after Rāma Prasāda (1912, Sacred Books of the Hindus IV) — the primary word-gloss source (a verified correction to the common assumption: Rāma Prasāda gives per-word glosses; Vivekananda does not).
  • Whole-sūtra translations after J. H. Woods (1914, Harvard Oriental Series 17), the philologically precise control text — labelled "Woods (1914)" when verbatim and "after Woods (1914)" when his archaic transliteration was modernised.

Book III carries dual numbering (195 vulgate ↔ 196 inclusive) with a toggle, and the disputed variant sūtra is badged where it sits; Book IV carries Bhoja's number where it diverges.
