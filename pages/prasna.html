Praśna — Indian horary & the KP sub-lords

Astronomy — verified Doctrine — historical Predictive validity — none demonstrated

Praśna ("the question") is the Indian tradition's answer to the same problem Western horary addresses: read a question from the sidereal chart of the moment it is asked. The praśna lagna stands for the querent and is judged exactly as a birth lagna (Praśna Mārga I.47); the Moon is the querent's mind and its house is itself a testimony; the Moon's nakṣatra is a data layer with no Western analogue. Layered on top is KP (Krishnamurti Paddhati): each nakṣatra cut into nine subs proportional to the Vimśottarī years — a self-verifiable table of exactly 249 entries, from which the querent's chosen horary number fixes the ascendant.

Honest note — please read Praśna and KP, like all astrology, have no demonstrated predictive validity. The arithmetic here is real and reproducible — the sidereal chart runs on the same verified engine as every other tool, and the 249-entry sub-lord table is generated from first principles and checks itself (exactly 249 entries, exactly 6 rāśi-boundary splits, arcs that tile the zodiac without gap or overlap). The judgement rules are those of the Ṣaṭpañcāśikā (6th c.), the Daivajña Vallabha and the Praśna Mārga (Kerala, 1649), each testimony cited to its verse, with edition disagreements flagged in-data — reproduced for study, never as advice. See Sources & Science.

The moment defaults to now — the classical praśna is judged for the instant the question reaches the astrologer. The KP number, when given, fixes the lagna from the querent's chosen sub-arc instead of the horizon (KP Reader VI); leave it blank for the plain time-chart.

The Indian mirror of Western horary

This page and the Master Tool's Lilly horary read the same moment with two independent grammars — and they are never merged. Cast the same date, time and place in both and compare: the tropical/Regiomontanus chart will put the planets in different signs and houses than the sidereal whole-sign chart (the ~24° ayanāṁśa shift guarantees it), Lilly judges by aspect perfection between significators (translation and collection of light), praśna by bhāva fortification and occupancy; Lilly's Moon is co-significator, praśna's Moon is the querent's mind and its house a verdict in itself. Where the two disagree, that disagreement is historically instructive — two traditions answering the same question from the same sky by different rules, neither with any demonstrated validity.

How this works

The 249-entry KP sub-lord table — generated, not copied

The zodiac is 27 nakṣatras of 13°20′ (48 000″), lords cycling Ketu→Venus→Sun→Moon→Mars→Rāhu→Jupiter→Saturn→Mercury from Aśvinī at 0° sidereal Aries. Each nakṣatra is cut into nine subs proportional to the Vimśottarī daśā years (sub arc = years × 400″ — Ketu 46′40″, Venus 2°13′20″, Sun 40′, Moon 1°06′40″, Mars 46′40″, Rāhu 2°, Jupiter 1°46′40″, Saturn 2°06′40″, Mercury 1°53′20″; the nine sum to exactly 13°20′), the sequence starting from the nakṣatra's own lord. That gives 27 × 9 = 243 arcs; the six rāśi boundaries that fall mid-sub (0° Taurus, Cancer, Virgo, Scorpio, Capricorn, Pisces) split their sub into two numbered entries — the Rāhu sub in the Sun-ruled boundary nakṣatras, the Moon sub in the Jupiter-ruled ones — and 243 + 6 = 249. The Mars-ruled boundary nakṣatras never split: Mars+Rāhu+Jupiter+Saturn arcs sum to exactly 6°40′, so the sign line falls precisely on a sub junction. The whole table is integer arc-second arithmetic — every boundary exact, every published row reproducible (verified against the JyotishPortal 1–249 chart; where published tables print a row start 1″ high, that is an anti-overlap display convention, flagged).

The horary number & the sub-lord of a degree

In KP horary (KP Reader VI) the querent — not the astrologer — picks a number 1–249; that entry's sub-arc fixes the ascendant (degree taken at the start of the arc — an implemented convention, flagged), while the planets stand for the moment of judgment. For any sidereal longitude the engine walks the same table: sign lord → star lord (the nakṣatra's) → sub-lord. KP doctrine calls the cuspal sub-lord the "final judge" of a house's matter; that claim is reported, not endorsed. Two conventions are flagged rather than hidden: KP proper uses Placidus cusps and Krishnamurti's ayanāṁśa (~5–6′ from the Lahiri used here — enough to flip a sub-lord near a boundary); this page reckons cusps whole-sign from the lagna, the convention of this site's Vedic wing.

The praśna judgement — a cited, verified ruleset

The chart is judged as a nativity (PM I.47). Benefics and malefics follow Bṛhat Jātaka II.5: malefics are the waning Moon, the Sun, Mars, Saturn and Mercury joined with any of these; benefics are Jupiter, Venus, the waxing Moon and unafflicted Mercury (the benefic list comes from the translator notes, not the verse — flagged; Rāhu/Ketu are malefic only under the later Phaladīpikā layer, flagged). The master rule (ṢPŚ I.4): a benefic in the lagna, a benefic varga rising, or a śīrṣodaya sign rising (the clause most summaries drop) gives success; a malefic gives failure; mixed gives success with difficulty. The kendra configuration (DV II.20/21 & III.3): benefics in quadrants and trines with malefics in 3/6/11 — and not in the angles or the 8th (the exclusion clause shorter quotations truncate). The Moon's house (DV III.2; the 8th as obstruction per PM XVII.15, genre-dependent and flagged), bhāva fortification (ṢPŚ I.3), the retrograde 7th lord as the return of the absent (DV IV.10, IX.5), and the corrected lost-item rule (ṢPŚ I.5: the full Moon in the lagna aspected by Jupiter or Venus — not merely "a strong Moon"). Every testimony in the table above carries its verse.

What is honestly out of scope

The texts demand layers no engine can compute: nimittas (omens observed from the moment the astrologer leaves home), the breath (svara) examination, the Kerala tāmbūla (betel-leaf) and aṣṭamaṅgala gold-coin rituals, and the diviner's steady mind (PM II.12–15). The ārūḍha — the sign of the compass direction the querent faces (PM II.7–11) — is computable only if that physical datum is supplied, and is never inferred here. These are listed with the judgement, described as history.
