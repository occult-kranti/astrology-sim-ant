About, Sources & Scientific Context

This project is a study edition: faithful to Lilly's text, honest about the science, and transparent about its methods. Contents: Lilly · the book & editions · read it free · the modern revival · the science · internal debates · technical notes · sources.

William Lilly (1602–1681)

Born 1 May 1602 at Diseworth, Leicestershire, the son of a yeoman, Lilly walked to London in 1620 and rose from servant to the foremost astrologer of his age. He took up the art in 1632, and from 1641 practised professionally, casting (by his own account) close to two thousand questions a year. His annual almanacs under the byline Merlinus Anglicus (from 1644) sold tens of thousands of copies and made him a real force in the propaganda of the English Civil War, in which he sided with Parliament.

His coded “hieroglyphic” images of a burning city, published in Monarchy or No Monarchy (1651), led to his being examined by a parliamentary committee after the Great Fire of London in 1666 — he was discharged, with the help of his friend Elias Ashmole. He died at Hersham, Surrey, on 9 June 1681. His candid autobiography, addressed to Ashmole, was published in 1715.

The book and its editions

Christian Astrology, Modestly Treated of in Three Books (London, 1647) was the first comprehensive textbook of astrology written in English rather than Latin. It was printed by Thomas Brudenell for John Partridge and Humphrey Blunden. A second edition, “corrected and amended,” followed in 1659 (printed by John Macock) — substantively a corrected reprint.

  • The originals (1647 / 1659) use 17th-century orthography, including the long-s (ſ) — authoritative but slow to read.
  • Regulus Publishing (1985), championed by Olivia Barclay and produced by Clive Kavan, reset the complete text in modern type with facsimile tables — the edition that launched the modern revival.
  • Astrology Classics (ed. David R. Roell, 2005) and Deborah Houlding's Ascella edition (1999) modernise the spelling and add glossaries, notes and redrawn charts; both split the work into Books I–II and Book III.
  • The 19th-century Zadkiel abridgement (An Introduction to Astrology, 1852) is heavily cut and is not a reliable representation of Lilly's text; it even substituted a Placidus table for Lilly's Regiomontanus one.

Where modern editors or later authors diverge from Lilly, this site follows what Lilly actually printed and flags the difference (his Ptolemaic terms; his two-ruler triplicities with Mars ruling Water day and night; his orb values).

A flagged fork — the Part of Fortune. This site computes the Part of Fortune as Ascendant + Moon − Sun for both day and night charts (the formula Lilly prints). This is a genuinely contested point: the older Ptolemaic rule reverses it by night to Ascendant + Sun − Moon. Because the Part of Fortune is one of the candidates for the hyleg, the choice can affect the length-of-life reading downstream — so we flag it rather than present it as settled.

The modern traditional revival

Lilly is the single most important English-language source for the late-20th-century revival of traditional astrology. Olivia Barclay (1919–2001) championed the 1985 facsimile and founded the Qualifying Horary Practitioner course; Deborah Houlding created the Skyscript archive and an annotated edition; John Frawley (who trained with Barclay) follows Lilly closely in The Horary Textbook; Sue Ward is the careful scholar of Lilly's method and the “considerations.” The wider recovery of older sources — Project Hindsight (Schmidt, Hand, Zoller, 1993) and Chris Brennan's Hellenistic work — restored the techniques Lilly inherited.

The scientific assessment — read this carefully

The bottom line Astrology — including horary and natal astrology — has no demonstrated predictive validity and no plausible physical mechanism. It is classified by the scientific community as a pseudoscience. The persuasiveness of readings is well explained by ordinary psychology, not by the planets.
  • The Carlson double-blind test (1985). Shawn Carlson, “A double-blind test of astrology,” Nature 318: 419–425. Designed with astrologers (nominated by the NCGR), it found they could match natal charts to personality profiles no better than chance — “clearly refut[ing] the astrological hypothesis.” (Later fringe re-analyses claim a tiny effect by changing the pre-registered method; they have not overturned the mainstream reading.)
  • The “time-twins” study (2003). Geoffrey Dean & Ivan Kelly followed more than 2,000 people born within days of one another in London in 1958; across 100+ variables they found no shared traits or fates — “the results are uniformly negative.”
  • The Barnum/Forer effect (1949). People rate a single generic personality sketch — Forer's was assembled from a newsstand astrology column — as a strikingly accurate description of themselves (≈4.3/5). This is the leading explanation for why readings feel true.
  • No mechanism. The gravitational and electromagnetic influences of the planets at birth are vanishingly small — far weaker than those of the attending midwife or the delivery-room lights.
  • The “Mars effect” (Gauquelin) — the most famous claimed empirical support — failed independent replication and is attributed to data-selection bias.
  • Jung's marriage experiment (1952) — the one formal test run by astrology's most sympathetic modern investigator. C. G. Jung tallied the cross-aspects of 483 married couples' horoscopes, hoping the traditional "marriage aspects" would beat chance. They did not: the physicist Markus Fierz's correction showed the result was ordinary noise, and Jung himself said so in print. It is a model of honest science — and a believer's own null result. The site's Jung wing reconstructs it and re-runs the null model live.

None of this diminishes Lilly's historical importance, the internal elegance of his system, or the genuine pleasure of learning it. We present his astrology as a cultural and intellectual artefact — and the calculations, which are real astronomy, as verifiably correct. We simply do not claim the interpretations have predictive power, because the evidence says they do not.

Debates within the tradition

  • House systems. Lilly used Regiomontanus (this site's default for horary). Most modern Western astrology defaults to Placidus; the Hellenistic revival favours whole-sign. All four are offered.
  • The outer planets. Uranus (1781), Neptune (1846) and Pluto (1930) were unknown to Lilly. This is a strictly seven-planet site, as his work is — we note where moderns assign them rulerships.
  • The lunar nodes. Lilly himself disagreed with “the Ancients,” finding the North Node benefic and the South Node malefic. We use the mean node, as the tradition did.

Technical & editorial notes

All charts are computed in your browser — there is no server. Planetary positions come from astronomy-engine (truncated VSOP87, MIT-licensed, ≈1 arc-minute), as geocentric apparent ecliptic longitudes in the true equinox of date (the tropical zodiac). The Ascendant, Midheaven and the Regiomontanus, Placidus, whole-sign and equal house cusps are computed here and validated against published reference vectors.

Validation: mean obliquity matches the standard value to five decimal places; the Sun falls on 0° Aries at the March equinox and 0° Cancer at the June solstice; it sits on the Midheaven at local apparent noon; the twelve Regiomontanus cusps come out strictly in zodiacal order; and the dignity tables reproduce Lilly's 1647 text (every term-row sums to 30°). Historical caveat: Lilly worked in Old-Style (Julian) dates and local apparent time; the calculators use the modern Gregorian calendar and a UTC offset.

The golden example case — reproduce it yourself. The test suite runs one fixed, externally-verifiable scenario through the entire engine: the J2000 epoch (1 Jan 2000, 12:00 UT) at London, with C. G. Jung's published nativity as the birth moment and a 7th-house question. Click it and check the figures against any almanac: the Sun stands at 10°22′ Capricorn (the textbook J2000 solar longitude, 280.37°), the Lahiri ayanāṁśa reads 23°51′, Jung's chart shows its published Leo Sun / Taurus Moon / early-Aquarius Ascendant, his profected age of 124 activates the 5th house (124 mod 12 = 4), and the Vedic Sarvāṣṭakavarga sums to its invariant 337. The same case runs headlessly in scripts/engine-test.mjs on every change — the mathematics is the checkable part of this site, and this is how we keep it checkable.
