Cast a chart by hand — the ephemeris & tables method

Before the software, an astrologer cast a chart with three books on the desk — a midnight ephemeris, an atlas of time-zone history, and a book of house tables — and a page of arithmetic. This tool walks that classic workflow in five stations, exactly as Catherine Urban's Chart Calculations for the Apocalypse workshop and The Astrology Podcast ep. 396 teach it — and it checks every hand step against this site's verified engine and shows you the delta. The deltas are the lesson: they reveal where the old book rules diverge from the true sky, and by how astonishingly little.

That the finished chart then means anything about a life is the separate, interpretive claim, and it has no demonstrated validity. How this works — the five stations, and why the deltas matter

Station 1 — the atlas step is where charts actually break

The trigonometry is never the problem; the time zone is. The American Atlas (Shanks) exists because US zone and daylight-saving history cannot be reconstructed from memory: year-round "War Time" 1942–45, Chicago hospitals recording births in Standard Time until 1959, and 1961–66 when — before the Uniform Time Act — DST was decided town by town ("in 1965 there were 23 different pairs of start and end dates in Iowa alone"). This tool takes the UTC offset you supply and does not ship a zone atlas; the honest move is to say so and point at where the real risk lives. Ep. 396's diagnostic: when two calculators disagree, "usually the reason is a time-zone issue where one is calculating Daylight Saving Time and the other is saying that it wasn't."

Station 2 — sidereal time and the acceleration constant

Sidereal time gains on solar time by about 3m 56.6s per solar day, so over any UT interval the sky's clock runs a little fast — the "acceleration." The hand-books teach the round rule 10 seconds per hour of clock time, computed as (hours × 10 + minutes ÷ 6) seconds; the exact mean rate is 9.8565 s/hour (236.5554 s of sidereal gain ÷ 24). On the worked vector the book rule costs only +1.8 s of sidereal time over a 12.5-hour interval — about 0.45′ on the Midheaven, harmless for any sign or house placement, and exactly the kind of delta this page is built to show. (A third figure, 9.8333 s/h, is an imprecise rounding of the daily gain to 3m 56s — flagged, never used.) The longitude term converts arc to time at 15° per hour, East adding and West subtracting; Watson's own arithmetic in ep. 396 does it in seconds: degrees × 3600 + minutes × 60, ÷ 15.

Station 3 — interpolation and the proportional logarithm

Linear interpolation is just pos = midnight-position + daily-motion × interval ÷ 24. The proportional logarithm, plog(x) = log10(1440 ÷ x-in-minutes), is the classical labour-saver: because motion = daily-motion × interval ÷ 1440 min, plog(daily-motion) + plog(interval) = plog(the required motion), so two look-ups and one addition replace a multiplication. It is a computational shortcut, not an accuracy gain — both routes give the same answer, and both inherit the same one-day-interpolation error. That error is negligible for the Sun (≈0.00′ here) but reaches ~2.7′ for the Moon, whose motion curves within the day; the classics say so, and this page marks the Moon's row. We reproduce DeVore's printed table values exactly (plog 14°27′ = .22034, plog 7h35m = .50035, sum .72069 → 4°34′) as the historical check.

Station 4 — house cusps by double interpolation

The Book of Tables is indexed by sidereal time down the rows (one row per 4 minutes of sidereal time, which is exactly 1° of right ascension of the Midheaven) and by latitude across the columns. You read the four surrounding cells and interpolate bilinearly. This page generates those four cells from the engine itself (holding the birth date's obliquity fixed) rather than a scanned page, so the demo is honest about its provenance — and the double-interpolated Ascendant lands about 0.05′ from the engine's exact value. The physical Michelsen Book of Tables tabulates both Placidus and Koch; this engine implements Placidus (the system Urban's workshop teaches), so the demo is Placidus-only and says so.

Station 5 — why teach a skill software has retired

Watson and Urban teach hand calculation for the NCGR Level I exam and for the "embodied connection with the chart" it builds — while freely admitting this is "the first generation of astrologers in history who don't need to know how to calculate charts in order to practice astrology." Our thesis is narrower and testable: every hand step here is checked against the exact engine with the delta shown. The deltas are the pedagogy, not an embarrassment — they teach precisely how good, and how limited, each historical rule is.

A strong, honest caveat Everything on this page is real mathematics, and it is correct. What it produces is a map of the sky at a moment — accurate, verifiable, and silent about the person born under it. The step from that map to a claim about character or fate is the interpretive move, and it has never been shown to work. Learn the calculation for the craft and the history; keep the honesty about what it can and cannot tell you. See Sources & Science.
