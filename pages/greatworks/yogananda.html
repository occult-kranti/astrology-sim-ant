Paramahansa Yogananda — Autobiography of a Yogi

The century's best-known spiritual memoir is this wing's hardest honest-framing case. Yogananda's 1946 first edition (48 chapters) is a devotional narrative dense with reported miracles — bilocation, materializations, a resurrection, a woman said to live without food, an astrological gem-armlet. Here every one of those episodes is stored as the book's claim ("Yogananda recounts…", "the book claims…"), page-cited to the first edition, never endorsed. The one astrology chapter — ch. 16, "Outwitting the Stars" — maps to the site's vedic-remedies museum piece: the gem-remedy is described, never asserted to work. And the public-domain story is itself a chapter of the method: the 1946 first edition is US public domain, while every revised SRF edition is copyrighted.

Miracle chapters are the author's claims — reported, never endorsed The Autobiography prescribes no ritual, but it narrates the supernatural on nearly every page. This wing takes no position on any of it: each episode (chs. 3, 5, 7, 13, 18, 19, 32, 33, 34, 39, 43, 46 and the rest) is catalogued as the book's claim, with a first-edition chapter cite, exactly as the wing frames the "powers" of the Yoga Sūtras — described side by side, as claims. The historicity of the deathless guru "Babaji" is contested and left unresolved.

"Kriya Yoga" (Yogananda's lineage) is not "kriyā-yoga" (Yoga Sūtra II.1) Required disambiguation. Yogananda's "Kriya Yoga" (ch. 26) is a specific prāṇāyāma breath-technique of his Babaji → Lahiri Mahasaya → Sri Yukteswar lineage. Patañjali's kriyā-yoga, in Yoga Sūtra II.1, is the triad tapas, svādhyāya, īśvarapraṇidhāna (discipline, self-study, devotion to Īśvara). Same words, two different things — a collision, not an identity. See the glossary disambiguation, the Yoga Sūtras II.1 treatment, and the parallel note on Vivekananda's Raja Yoga.

The public domain, as a case study — recorded neutrally In Self-Realization Fellowship Church v. Ananda Church of Self-Realization, 206 F.3d 1322 (9th Cir. 2000), the court held that Yogananda's books were not works made for hire and that a non-author proprietor could not renew the book copyrights — so SRF's 1974 renewal of the 1946 edition was ineffective and the 1946 first edition is US public domain (Project Gutenberg #7452). Only the 1946 first edition is quote-safe; every later SRF revision (added ch. 49, new photographs) is copyrighted and cite-only. The 2002 final judgment split the wider dispute — SRF retained magazine articles, recordings and unpublished writings; Ananda won the photographs. The litigation is recorded as court outcome (fact) plus each party's position (position), neither party's org endorsed.
