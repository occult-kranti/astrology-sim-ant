Swami Vivekananda — Raja Yoga

Raja Yoga (1896) is how most Anglophone readers first met Patañjali: Vivekananda's lectures plus his own "rather free translation" and commentary on the Yoga Sūtras, framed as an experimental "science of the mind." The site's Yoga Sūtras wing treats the sūtras directly, so this book is cross-linked to that wing pāda by pāda as his rendering — a reception layer, labelled as his interpretation, never mistaken for the primary text. Everything published in his lifetime (d. 1902) is US public domain; the modern Complete Works resets carry cite-only posthumous matter. His efficacy claims (that the graded practice awakens kuṇḍalinī and yields samādhi and powers) are reported as the text's claims; his prāṇa/ākāśa "physics" is flagged as 1890s vitalism.

Two things kept honest His yoga is a rendering, not the sūtras. Vivekananda calls his Part II a "rather free translation"; every cross-link into the Yoga Sūtras wing labels it as his interpretation. The scholarship is contested, both ways. Elizabeth De Michelis's "Modern Yoga" thesis — that his yoga fused Patañjali with Neo-Vedānta, Brahmo reform and Western occult currents — sits against traditionalist readings that treat it as faithful transmission; the site presents both and resolves neither.

"Kriyā-yoga" here is not Yogananda's "Kriya Yoga" Vivekananda's Part II.2 renders Patañjali's kriyā-yoga — the Yoga Sūtra II.1 triad of tapas, svādhyāya, īśvarapraṇidhāna (discipline, self-study, devotion to Īśvara). This is a different thing from the later Lahiri Mahasaya / Yogananda-lineage "Kriya Yoga", a specific prāṇāyāma breath-technique. The shared word is a collision, not an identity — see the glossary disambiguation and the Yoga Sūtras II.1 treatment.
