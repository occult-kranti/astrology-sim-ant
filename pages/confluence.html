The Great Confluence — a map of books and their journeys

Nine traditions, thirty-five centuries, one time axis. Every mark is a text, a person, an event, a translation or an institution; every arc is a documented journey — a book carried over a border, put into a new language, argued with, absorbed. This map plots influence, never validity: it shows that the Picatrix reached Latin, not that its talismans work; that the Upaniṣads reached Persian, not that Vedānta is physics. Who read whom is real history — and it is astonishing enough. Prefer the story told in order? Read the Hermetic Chronology.

Honest note — please read Every entry and every arc carries an epistemic label: documented transmission is kept visibly distinct from disputed readings, debunked claims, and conspiracy narratives — which stay on the map, drawn faint, because the record of what people falsely believed is also history. Dates for ancient texts are scholarly estimates; the map draws that uncertainty as soft bands, not sharp points, and the timeline's scale stretches as it nears the present — each era band says by how much. The arcs mean “demonstrably read, rendered or answered,” per the citation on each — influence is the claim, and the only claim.

How to read this map

Lanes = traditions

Columns run west of the spine to east. The gold-framed centre lane is the confluence — moments that belong to more than one tradition. A lane's colour is a finding aid; the label is the fact.

Shapes = kind

▮ text · ● person · ◆ event · ◎ translation · ⌂ institution. Shape carries the kind; colour carries the epistemic label — two separate channels, each also written in the drawer.

Marks = certainty

A crisp mark is dated to the year. A soft band behind it means a decade or a century of scholarly estimate; a long capsule means composed over a span (the mark sits at the start); ⚑ means the sources disagree — hover to read both sides.

Arcs = journeys

Arcs flow down the page, with time: solid = translation or influence, dashed = commentary or adaptation, dotted = refutation, a bead = synthesis. Hover any book to light up its travels; press t to follow a thread. Debunked and conspiracy entries are drawn faint on purpose — present, labelled, and never dressed as the documented record. Use the label chips to isolate them.

The four labels

documented established by primary texts or peer-reviewed scholarship · disputed contested or unsettled · debunked disproven by evidence · conspiracy no evidentiary basis, shown as discourse.

    The atlas is a grid of entries you can walk with the keyboard. Arrow up and down move to the previous or next entry in the same tradition; arrow left and right move to the nearest entry in the neighbouring tradition. Home and End jump to the first and last entry; Page Up and Page Down jump between eras. Press Enter to open an entry's detail; Escape to close it. Press t to follow a transmission thread, then n and p to step through its stops. Press slash to search the map. A minimap beside the atlas mirrors these controls for pointer users.

    Method note A curated overview, not an exhaustive register. Lane order and the era-warped scale are editorial choices made for legibility and disclosed on the map itself — each era band states its own scale, and the arrows mean “demonstrably read, rendered or answered,” per the citation on each arc. Influence is the claim, and the only claim; none of these systems has any demonstrated predictive validity. Techniques named in an entry are described, never prescribed, and where a source records a genuine hazard (an alchemical or rasa elixir's toxicity) that note is kept. See Sources & Science.
