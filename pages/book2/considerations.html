Considerations before Judgement

Before reading a horary chart, Lilly “gives warning to the Astrologer” to consider whether the figure is radical — fit to be judged. These are cautions for reflection, not mechanical rules that forbid a reading.

“The Question then shall be taken for radicall, or fit to be judged, when as the Lord of the hour … and the Lord of the Ascendant or first House, are of one Triplicity, or be one, or of the same nature.” — Lilly, Christian Astrology, Book II

The considerations

The Ascendant in early degrees

If the first three degrees of a sign ascend, “the matter is not yet ripe for judgement” — unless the querent is very young or the matter genuinely new.

The Ascendant in late degrees

If 27°–29° ascend, “it's no wayes safe to give judgement” — the matter may be already decided, or out of the querent's hands. (Lilly allows an exception when the querent's age matches the degree.)

The Moon void of course

When the Moon makes no further aspect before leaving her sign, “all manner of matters go hardly on … yet somewhat she performs if void of course, and be in Taurus, Cancer, Sagittarius or Pisces.”

The Moon in the Via Combusta

The “combust way”, from 15° Libra to 15° Scorpio. Lilly counsels care when the Moon is here.

Saturn in the 1st house

“If Saturn be in the Ascendant, especially Retrograde, the matter of that Question seldome or never comes to good.”

Saturn in the 7th house

Saturn (or a corrupted lord of the 7th) here “corrupts the judgement of the Astrologer” — the 7th being the astrologer's own house in a consultation. Judge with humility.

Lord of the Ascendant combust

“If the Lord of the Ascendant be combust, neither will the question propounded take, nor the Querent be regulated.”

Radicality of the hour

When the planet ruling the hour agrees in triplicity or nature with the lord of the Ascendant, it is a sign the figure is sound and fit to judge.

The modern scholarly view Deborah Houlding and others stress that Lilly never meant the considerations as automatic “stop” signs. His own casebooks show him judging charts in which considerations were present. They are prompts to pause and weigh the figure's reliability — which is exactly how the calculator reports them: as advisories, with severity, not as a verdict.
