Foundations — Book I

Essential dignity & the almuten

Essential dignity measures how "at home" a planet is at a given zodiac longitude, by the five Ptolemaic rulerships Lilly tabulates: domicile, exaltation, triplicity, term, and face. Each gives a planet a fixed number of points (and detriment/fall subtract points), so a positive score means strength and a negative score means weakness. The almuten of a degree answers a related question: of the seven traditional planets, which one holds the most essential dignity at that exact point — i.e. who is the rightful "lord" of that degree.

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. Find the sign and the degree-within-sign from the longitude (signOf(lon) gives sign index 0=Aries…11=Pisces and the degree 0–30).
  2. Domicile (+5): if the planet is the domicile lord of that sign (from the DOMICILE table, e.g. Aries→Mars).
  3. Exaltation (+4): if the planet's exaltation sign matches (whole-sign, the exact degree is stored but not required for the point), e.g. Sun→19° Aries, Moon→3° Taurus, Saturn→21° Libra.
  4. Triplicity (+3): using Lilly's two-ruler (day/night) scheme, award if isDay and the planet is the day ruler, or night and it is the night ruler. Fire = Sun/Jupiter, Earth = Venus/Moon, Air = Saturn/Mercury, Water = Mars/Mars.
  5. Term (+2): look up the Ptolemaic term containing the degree (termRulerAt: matches where start ≤ deg < end; the five terms per sign sum to 30°). Sun and Moon never rule terms.
  6. Face (+1): the 10°-wide decan ruler (faceRulerAt = floor(deg/10), capped at 2), in continuous Chaldean order starting with Mars at 0° Aries.
  7. Debilities: Detriment (−5) if the planet rules the opposite sign; Fall (−4) if the sign is opposite the planet's exaltation.
  8. Peregrine (−5): only if the planet has NO positive dignity above AND is not already in detriment or fall (so a debility is never double-counted).
  9. Sum every row to get the net total.
  10. Almuten: run the essential-dignity scoring for all seven planets (Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Sun, Venus, Mercury, Moon) at that longitude, sum only the positive dignity points for each, and pick the highest scorer (ties resolved by first-found in that planet order).

Worked example

Take a planet at 10° Libra (longitude 190°) in a daytime chart, and compute the almuten of that degree. 10° Libra falls in Libra's third term and second face.

Dignity at 10° Libra (day)RulerPoints
Domicile (Libra)Venus+5
Exaltation (Libra)Saturn (21° Libra)+4
Triplicity (Air, day)Saturn+3
Term (6°–11° Libra)Venus+2

Tallying positive dignity per planet at this degree:

  • Saturn: exaltation +4, triplicity +3, face +1 = 8
  • Venus: domicile +5, term +2 = 7
  • All other planets: 0

The almuten of 10° Libra (day) is Saturn with 8 points, narrowly beating Venus (7). Note the almuten can differ from the simple domicile lord (Venus here), because exaltation + triplicity + face stack up for Saturn. In a night chart the Air triplicity ruler becomes Mercury, so Saturn would drop to 5 and Venus's 7 would make Venus the almuten instead.

Accidental dignity & chart health (the green/amber/red verdict)

Where essential dignity asks "is this planet strong by sign?", accidental dignity asks "is it well-placed and well-conditioned in this particular chart?" — by house, by speed and direction, by nearness to the Sun, and by conjunction with the three table fixed stars. The tool adds up the resulting fortitudes and debilities into a per-planet ledger (Lilly's own method), and then a separate chart-health engine distils those afflictions, plus the Moon's condition and Lilly's Considerations, into a single green / amber / red verdict that tells you, at a glance, how clean or impeded the whole figure is.

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. House placement. Score the planet by the house it occupies from a fixed table: angular 1st and 10th = +5; 7th and 4th and 11th = +4; 2nd and 5th = +3; 9th = +2; 3rd = +1; and the dark houses 8th = −2, 6th = −2, 12th = −5.
  2. Direction & speed (the five non-luminaries only; nodes and Fortune skipped). Retrograde = −5, else direct = +4. Compare the planet's speed to its mean daily motion (Saturn 0.034, Jupiter 0.083, Mars 0.524, Venus 1.2, Mercury 1.383 °/day): faster than mean = swift +2, slower = slow −2. The Moon is scored swift/slow against mean 13.176 °/day but never retrograde.
  3. Moon's light. Take the elongation = (Moon − Sun) mod 360. Under 180° she is increasing (waxing) +2; otherwise decreasing (waning) −2.
  4. Solar proximity (every planet except the Sun, nodes, Fortune). Measure the shortest separation from the Sun. Within 0°17′ (0.283°) = Cazimi +5; else within 8°30′ (8.5°) = Combust −5; else within 17° = under the Sun's beams −4; else free of the beams +5.
  5. Fixed-star conjunctions. Test the three table stars at their 1647 longitudes — Regulus 144° (+6), Spica 198° (+5), Caput Algol 50° (−5). Orb is 2°, except Algol which uses a 5° orb. Within orb, add that star's score.
  6. Sum the ledger. The accidental total is the sum of all rows above. (Essential dignity is computed separately: domicile +5, exaltation +4, triplicity +3, term +2, face +1; detriment −5, fall −4, peregrine −5.)
  7. Distil afflictions per planet (chart-health engine, the seven planets). Each affliction is tagged with a severity: combust / besieged by both malefics = bad; under-beams, retrograde, cadent (3/6/9/12), peregrine = caution; slow = note; cazimi = good. Besieged = a non-malefic aspecting both Mars and Saturn with the configuration hemmed (separating from one while applying to the other, or applying to both).
  8. Add Moon & chart-wide signals. Append the Moon's condition (waxing/waning, swift vs. slow under 12°/day "slow as Saturn", under-beams, new-moon, eclipse-season near a node within 12° at syzygy, applying hard aspect to a malefic) and Lilly's Considerations advisories. Sort all by severity.
  9. The verdict. Count bad and caution severities across every global and per-planet flag. RED if bad ≥ 1 OR caution ≥ 3 ("strongly impeded"). Else AMBER if caution ≥ 1 ("some cautions stand"). Else GREEN ("few impediments — a clean figure").

Worked example

Take Mars at 22° Taurus 00′ (longitude 52°), retrograde, moving 0.30°/day, placed in the 12th house, with the Sun at 25° Taurus (longitude 55°), in a day chart.

Now the chart-health distillation of this one planet yields three flags: Combust = bad, Retrograde = caution, Cadent (12th) = caution (and slow = a "note"). With a single bad already present, the rule bad ≥ 1 fires immediately:

  • Verdict: RED — "Strongly impeded — judge or elect with great care."

Even if Mars were not combust, the two cautions (retrograde + cadent) plus any one more caution elsewhere — three cautions total — would also push the figure to RED; a single caution alone would be AMBER.

Part of Fortune & antiscia

The Part (Lot) of Fortune is a calculated point — not a planet — that carries the Moon's relationship to the Sun forward from the Ascendant. Traditionally it marks the body, livelihood, and material wellbeing of the native. Antiscia are the "shadow" points a degree casts when reflected across the solstice axis (0° Cancer–0° Capricorn); two planets in antiscion behave as if hidden in aspect — they share the same declination and answer the question "where does this degree secretly connect?"

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. Read three ecliptic longitudes in degrees from the cast chart: the Ascendant (asc), the Sun's longitude, and the Moon's longitude.
  2. Part of Fortune: compute asc + moonLon − sunLon. The code uses this single formula for both day and night charts (it does not flip to Asc + Sun − Moon at night).
  3. Reduce the result into the 0–360° circle with norm360(x) = ((x % 360) + 360) % 360, so any negative or over-360 value wraps cleanly.
  4. Assign the resulting longitude to a chart point (Fortune, glyph ⊕) with latitude 0, speed 0, not retrograde, and look up its house with houseOf against the cusps.
  5. Antiscion of any point: compute norm360(360 − lon) — the mirror across the 0° Cancer / 0° Capricorn solstitial axis.
  6. Contra-antiscion: compute norm360(180 − lon) — the point diametrically opposite the antiscion (mirror across the 0° Aries / 0° Libra equinoctial axis).
  7. Note the day/night flag itself is set separately: isDay is true when the Sun's house is ≥ 7 (Sun above the horizon), but this flag is not applied to the Fortune formula in this code.

Worked example

Take a chart with Ascendant at 15° Taurus = 45°, Sun at 10° Gemini = 70°, Moon at 20° Leo = 140°.

Part of Fortune = asc + moonLon − sunLon = 45 + 140 − 70 = 115°. Since 115 is already in range, norm360(115) = 115° = 25° Cancer. That point is then placed in whatever house contains 115°.

A case that needs wrapping: Asc 300°, Sun 350°, Moon 40° gives 300 + 40 − 350 = −10, and norm360(−10) = 350° (20° Pisces).

Antiscia of the Sun at 70° (10° Gemini):

  • Antiscion = norm360(360 − 70) = 290° = 20° Capricorn.
  • Contra-antiscion = norm360(180 − 70) = 110° = 20° Cancer.

So a planet sitting near 20° Capricorn would be in antiscion to this Sun — a hidden conjunction by reflected degree.

Source: William Lilly, Christian Astrology (1647), Bk. I — Part of Fortune computed Asc + Moon − Sun (Lilly does not reverse it by sect); antiscia per CA Bk. I (solstitial mirror across Cancer/Capricorn). Reflected in code comments at assets/js/core/astro.js lines 202–214.

Planetary hours & the day ruler

Each day and night is split into twelve unequal "planetary hours," and every hour (plus the day itself) is governed by one of the seven planets. This tells you which planet rules right now — the answer to "is this a good hour to begin a Venus matter, a Saturn matter, and so on?" The planetary day starts at sunrise, not midnight, and the rulers cycle in the Chaldean order.

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. For the location (lat/lon) and date, find sunrise, the following sunset, and the next sunrise using the astronomy engine (SearchRiseSet on the Sun).
  2. Decide which planetary day the instant falls in: if the instant is at or after today's sunrise it belongs to today's day; if it is before sunrise it belongs to the previous day's night (the code steps back 24 hours and uses that day's sunrise/sunset).
  3. Split the daylight span into 12 equal day-hours of length (sunset − sunrise) / 12, and the night span into 12 equal night-hours of length (nextSunrise − sunset) / 12. These hours are generally not 60 minutes.
  4. Compute the hour index 0–23. Daytime: floor((instant − sunrise) / dayHourLen). Night: 12 + floor((instant − sunset) / nightHourLen). The result is clamped to 0–23.
  5. Find the day ruler from the weekday of the day-start sunrise, indexed into DAY_RULERS = [Sun, Moon, Mars, Mercury, Jupiter, Venus, Saturn] by getUTCDay() (0 = Sunday).
  6. The first hour of the day is always ruled by the day ruler. Locate the day ruler's position in the Chaldean order [Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Sun, Venus, Mercury, Moon], then step forward by the hour index, wrapping every 7: ruler = CHALDEAN[(startIdx + hourNo) % 7].
  7. Report the ruler, the day ruler, the 1-based hour number (hourNo + 1), whether it is a night hour, and the hour length in minutes.

Worked example

Take a Tuesday (getUTCDay() = 2). The day ruler is DAY_RULERS[2] = Mars — hence "Tuesday/Mars."

Suppose for that location sunrise is 06:00 and sunset is 18:00, so the daylight span is 12 hours. Each day-hour is (18:00 − 06:00) / 12 = 60 minutes (equal-length only because day and night happen to be 12h here). We want the ruler at 11:30.

  • Instant 11:30 is after sunrise → today's day, daytime branch.
  • Hour index = floor((11:30 − 06:00) / 60min) = floor(5.5) = 5 → the 6th hour (hourNumber = 6).
  • Day ruler Mars sits at index 2 in the Chaldean order.
  • ruler = CHALDEAN[(2 + 5) % 7] = CHALDEAN[0] = Saturn.

Sequence of the day from the 1st hour: Mars, Sun, Venus, Mercury, Moon, Saturn, Jupiter, … — confirming the 6th hour is Saturn.

Horary — Book II

Aspects, orbs & the modes of perfection

An aspect is a meaningful angular distance between two bodies (conjunction 0°, sextile 60°, square 90°, trine 120°, opposition 180°). Following Lilly, orbs belong to the planets, not the aspects: two bodies are "in aspect" when their distance from the exact angle is within the sum of their two moieties (half-orbs). The modes of perfection answer the horary question "will the matter come to pass, and how?" by looking at whether the two significators complete an aspect directly, or whether the light is carried, gathered, cut off, or withdrawn by a third planet.

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. Smallest separation. For longitudes a and b compute |((a − b + 540) mod 360) − 180|, giving a distance in 0–180°.
  2. Allowance = sum of moieties. Each planet has a full orb (Saturn 9, Jupiter 9, Mars 7, Sun 15, Venus 7, Mercury 7, Moon 12; nodes/Fortune 0); the moiety is half that. The allowance is moiety(A) + moiety(B).
  3. Test each aspect. For each of the five Ptolemaic angles, compute orbOff = |distance − angle|. If orbOff ≤ allowance, the bodies are in that aspect (only the first match is returned, scanned 0→60→90→120→180).
  4. Partile / exact flags. It is flagged exact when orbOff < 0.0167 × allowance (≈1% of the allowance), and partile when both bodies share the same whole degree of their sign and orbOff < 1°.
  5. Applying vs separating. Step both longitudes forward by dt = 0.05 (lon + speed × 0.05) and recompute orbOff. If the future orb is smaller, the aspect is applying; otherwise separating.
  6. Direct perfection. If the two significators A and B are in aspect at all, that is recorded as the direct mode.
  7. Translation of light. A lighter (faster) third planet C that is separating from one significator and applying to the other carries the light between them. Speed rank, fastest→slowest: Moon, Mercury, Venus, Sun, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn.
  8. Collection of light. A heavier (slower than both) third planet C to which both A and B are applying collects the light; "received" is true if C's longitude falls in dignities ruled by both A and B.
  9. Prohibition. While A and B apply, if a faster third planet C applies to one of them with a smaller orb than the A–B orb, it perfects first and cuts the matter off.
  10. Refranation. If A and B are applying but the significator about to perfect is retrograde, it withdraws (refrains).
  11. Timing. count = max(0.5, round(orbOff × 2)/2) degrees-to-exact (half-degree precision); the unit comes from a table keyed by the sign's mode (Movable/Common/Fixed) and the house type (Angular 1/4/7/10, Succedent 2/5/8/11, else Cadent) — e.g. Movable+Angular → days, Fixed+Succedent → years.

Worked example

Take the Moon at 10°00′ Gemini and Mars at 14°00′ Leo.

  • Gemini begins at 60°, so Moon = 70°; Leo begins at 120°, so Mars = 134°. Distance = |134 − 70| = 64°.
  • Moiety(Moon) = 12/2 = 6°; moiety(Mars) = 7/2 = 3.5°. Allowance = 9.5°.
  • Sextile angle = 60°, so orbOff = |64 − 60| = 4°. Since 4° ≤ 9.5°, they are in a sextile.
  • Exact? threshold = 0.0167 × 9.5 ≈ 0.159°; 4° > that, so not exact. Not partile (10 ≠ 14).
  • Moon moves faster than Mars and is approaching the trine point, so stepping forward shrinks the orb → applying.

If the Moon sits in the 3rd house (Cadent) and Gemini is a Common sign, the timing table gives the unit months; count = round(4 × 2)/2 = 4, so the reading is "about 4 months."

The Moon’s condition: void of course & the via combusta (Spica exception)

Before electing a moment for any work, the tradition weighs the Moon above all other testimonies. Two of her afflictions are checked here. Void of course asks: will the Moon complete another applying Ptolemaic aspect before she leaves her current sign? If not, she is "void" and the old maxim warns "nothing will come of the matter." The via combusta ("burning way") asks: is the Moon travelling the distressed arc between 15° Libra and 15° Scorpio? If so the work is troubled — unless she sits on the fortunate fixed star Spica (~24° Libra), the one "oasis" in that arc, which lifts the distress.

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. Void of course. Compute the Moon's degrees-to-sign-change: degLeft = 30 − degInSign.
  2. For each of Sun, Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, take the aspect between it and the Moon. If any aspect is applying and its orb ≤ degLeft + 0.5 (i.e. it will perfect before the Moon quits her sign), the Moon is not void. Otherwise she is void of course.
  3. Severity: if the void Moon is in Taurus, Cancer, Sagittarius or Pisces she "performs somewhat" → flagged only as a note; in any other sign it is a caution. In the election score a caution-level void costs −3 (bad), a note-level void −1.
  4. Via combusta zone. Normalise the Moon's longitude and test 195° ≤ lon ≤ 225° (15° Libra to 15° Scorpio). If outside, no via-combusta penalty.
  5. Spica exception. If in the zone, precess Spica's catalog longitude to the chart date: spica = 204.1 + (50.29″/3600) × (year − 2020), i.e. ~0.013969°/yr from its 2020 value of 204.1° (24°Lib06′).
  6. If the Moon is within the Spica orb (default spicaOrb = 2°) of that precessed longitude, set exemptBySpica → the distress is lifted (a note, +0, "the fortunate oasis").
  7. The via combusta is active = inZone && !exemptBySpica. An active via combusta costs −2 (caution) in the election score.

Worked example

Setup: Moon at 23°30′ Libra (longitude 203.5°), date 2026-06-25, electing a love operation.

Via combusta test:

  • 203.5° lies in 195–225° → inZone = true.
  • Spica precessed to 2026: 204.1 + (50.29/3600) × (2026 − 2020) = 204.1 + 0.013969 × 6 = 204.184°.
  • Separation: |203.5 − 204.184| = 0.68°, which is ≤ 2° orb → exemptBySpica = true.
  • Therefore active = inZone && !exemptBySpica = false. Result: a note — "in the Via Combusta but conjunct Spica — the distress is lifted," +0 to the score.

Void-of-course test: suppose the Moon (in late Libra) has only 6.5° left in her sign (degLeft = 30 − 23.5 = 6.5). She applies to a trine of Saturn with orb 4.0°. Since 4.0 ≤ 6.5 + 0.5 = 7.0, the aspect perfects before she leaves Libra → not void (no penalty). Had her nearest applying aspect been 9° off, 9 > 7.0 → void of course; in Libra (not an exception sign) that is a caution, costing −3.

Annual profections & the Lord of the Year

Annual profection is a traditional timing technique that "advances" the chart one whole sign for every completed year of life. The sign reached at a given age, and its domicile ruler, the Lord of the Year, tell you which house topics and which planet are activated for that year of the native's life. It answers: which part of my chart, and which planet, is in charge of this year?

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. Find the natal Ascendant's sign index with signOf(chart.asc).index (0 = Aries, 1 = Taurus, … 11 = Pisces).
  2. Take age as whole completed years since birth (an integer ≥ 0; year 0 is the birth year).
  3. Compute the profected sign index: (natalAscSign + age) % 12. Each year advances exactly one sign, completing the 12-sign circuit every 12 years.
  4. Compute the activated house: (age % 12) + 1, so age 0 → 1st house, age 1 → 2nd house, and so on (whole-sign houses from the Ascendant).
  5. Look up the Lord of the Year as the domicile ruler of the profected sign from the DOMICILE table, indexed by sign: Aries=Mars, Taurus=Venus, Gemini=Mercury, Cancer=Moon, Leo=Sun, Virgo=Mercury, Libra=Venus, Scorpio=Mars, Sagittarius=Jupiter, Capricorn=Saturn, Aquarius=Saturn, Pisces=Jupiter.
  6. Judge that lord's condition in the natal chart: essentialDignity(lord, lon, isDay).total for its essential score and accidentalDignity(lord, chart).total for its accidental score (both null if the planet is missing from the chart).
  7. Monthly profection (optional): starting from the annual profected sign, advance one sign per month, (profectedSignIndex + monthIndex) % 12, with monthIndex 0 = the annual sign; its domicile ruler is that month's lord.

Worked example

Native born with the Ascendant at 17° Scorpio; we want the Lord of the Year at age 34.

  • signOf(asc).index = floor(227 / 30) = 7 (Scorpio).
  • Profected sign index = (7 + 34) % 12 = 41 % 12 = 5 → Virgo.
  • Activated house = (34 % 12) + 1 = 10 + 1 = 11th house.
  • DOMICILE[5] = Mercury → Mercury is the Lord of the Year.

Primary directions (Naibod key)

A primary direction times an event by measuring the arc between a significator (a radical point of the chart — here the Ascendant or Midheaven) and a promissor (a planet that "promises" an event). That arc, measured in degrees, is converted into years of life by a time key. When the years lived equal the arc-in-years, the directed event comes to pass. It answers: at roughly what age does this planet "come to" the angle? Note: this module uses the simplified zodiacal (in-zodiac) arc — difference of ecliptic longitudes — not the rigorous mundane (Placidian semi-arc) method, which is deliberately out of scope.

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. Pick the time key. The default is naibod = 0.985647° of arc per year of life (the Sun's mean daily motion, ≈ 1° per year). The alternative is ptolemy = exactly 1° per year.
  2. Take the two significators (angles): the Ascendant (chart.asc) and the Midheaven (chart.mc).
  3. For each of the seven promissors in order — Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Sun, Venus, Mercury, Moon — read its ecliptic longitude.
  4. Compute the direct zodiacal arc: arc = norm360(planetLon − angleLon), i.e. promissor longitude minus significator longitude, normalised into the range 0–360° (forward succession of the signs).
  5. Convert the arc to years: years = arc / 0.985647 (Naibod). With the Ptolemy key it would be years = arc / 1.
  6. Keep only directions where 0 ≤ years ≤ maxYears (default maxYears = 90); discard anything beyond a lifetime.
  7. Sort all surviving directions ascending by years, so the earliest-fructifying direction comes first.

The directInZodiac helper additionally reports a converse arc (norm360(significatorLon − promissorLon)) and an unsigned shortest ecliptic arc (0–180°), but the angle-directions list uses only the direct arc above.

Worked example

Suppose the chart has Ascendant at 10° Aries = 10.0° and Mars at 25° Taurus = 55.0° longitude. Direct Mars (promissor) to the Ascendant (significator) with the Naibod key.

The solar return (revolution of the year)

The solar return — Lilly's "revolution of the year" — is the chart cast for the exact UTC instant each year when the transiting Sun returns to the very same tropical ecliptic longitude it held at your birth. It answers: what is the shape of the coming year? Because the Sun's return drifts about six hours later each year (and a whole day across leap years), the moment is found astronomically, not on the calendar birthday. The radix (natal chart) remains the perpetual significator; the revolution figure is judged for that year's accidents.

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. Take the natal Sun's tropical longitude from the radix (natalChart.planets.Sun.lon) and normalize it to [0, 360) as the target.
  2. Define a signed difference d(t) = angleDiff(sunLon(t), target), reduced to the range (−180°, 180°]. It is positive when the transiting Sun is "ahead" of its natal place and crosses zero exactly once per calendar year (the Sun moves ~0.985°/day, so there is one crossing).
  3. Set the scan window. With no hint, sweep the whole year (Jan 1 00:00 UTC of year to Jan 1 of year+1). If a birthday hint (aroundMonth/aroundDay) is given, search ±20 days around that date at noon UTC.
  4. Bracket the crossing by stepping day-by-day (86,400,000 ms): find the adjacent pair of days where sign(d) flips. A genuine crossing is a tiny step, so the flip is only accepted when |d(cur) − d(lo)| < 180° (this guards against the ±180° wrap).
  5. Bisect the bracketed interval up to 30 iterations, halving the time span each pass, until |d(mid)| < 0.0005° (well under sub-arcsecond). Return the midpoint Date as the return instant.
  6. Cast the revolution chart for that instant via castChart, using the natal latitude/longitude by default (overridable) and the Regiomontanus house system by default — Lilly's quadrant houses.
  7. Return { instant, chart, natalSunLon }. By construction the return chart's Sun matches the natal Sun to within 0.0005° because both use the same bodyPosition engine.

Worked example

Suppose a nativity with the natal Sun at 94.30° tropical (4°18′ Cancer), and we want the revolution for the year 2026, location London (51.51° N, 0.13° W).

  • Target = norm360(94.30°) = 94.30°.
  • Day-step bracket: on 2026-06-25 the Sun is near 93.9° → d ≈ −0.4°; on 2026-06-26 the Sun is near 94.9° → d ≈ +0.6°. The sign flips and |Δd| ≈ 1.0° < 180°, so the crossing is bracketed in [Jun 25, Jun 26].
  • Bisect: halving this ~1-day window repeatedly drives |d(mid)| below 0.0005° in roughly 20-something iterations, converging on an instant of about 2026-06-25 ~14:30 UTC (the Sun crossing 94.30°).
  • Cast: castChart builds the figure for that UTC instant at 51.51° N / 0.13° W in Regiomontanus houses.

Hyleg & alcocoden (the contested length-of-life)

The hyleg ("giver of life", Ptolemy's apheta) is the one chart point judged fit to set the native's vitality; the alcocoden ("giver of years", the oikodektor) is the planet that disposes that point, and its traditional planetary "years" estimate the span of life. This module answers: which of Sun, Moon, Ascendant, Part of Fortune, or the pre-natal syzygy is the hyleg, who rules its degree, and how many years does that ruler bestow? It is an openly contested technique — authors disagree on the aphetic houses, the order of examination, and whether dignity is required — so the code implements one clearly-scoped Lilly/Ptolemy reading and labels every assumption it makes.

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. Fix the aphetic (hylegiacal) houses. The code uses exactly the canonical five: HYLEGIACAL_HOUSES = [1, 7, 9, 10, 11]. The 8th (admitted by some authors) is deliberately excluded.
  2. Build the candidate list in sect order. If the chart is diurnal (chart.isDay): Sun, Moon, Ascendant, Part of Fortune, Syzygy. If nocturnal: Moon, Sun, Ascendant, Part of Fortune, Syzygy.
  3. Compute the pre-natal syzygy point. Measure elongation = Moon−Sun (mod 360). If < 180° the birth is waxing, so seek the last New Moon (conjunction, elongation crossing 0°); else the last Full Moon (opposition, crossing 180°). Scan backward in 1-day steps up to 31 days to bracket the sign change, then bisect 40 times (down to a 1-second tolerance). The syzygy point is the Moon's longitude at that instant. If it fails to bracket within 31 days, the Syzygy candidate is dropped and noted.
  4. Find each candidate's house with the cusp-span lookup require_houseOf(lon, cusps): for each house i, if norm360(lon − cusp[i]) < norm360(cusp[i+1] − cusp[i]) the point sits in house i. Flag it hylegiacal if that house is in {1,7,9,10,11}.
  5. Test dignity in place. For Sun or Moon: planetDignifiedAt — the luminary itself must hold some positive essential dignity (domicile / exaltation / triplicity / term / face, sect-aware) at its own degree (any one row with score > 0). For the abstract points (Ascendant, Part of Fortune, Syzygy): pointDignified — compute the degree's almuten and require almuten.score > 3 (i.e. more than the unavoidable term(2)+face(1) minimum, so a domicile/exaltation/triplicity ruler is present).
  6. Choose the hyleg (strict). Take the first candidate, in sect order, that is BOTH in a hylegiacal house AND dignified there.
  7. Fallback if none qualifies. If no candidate is both aphetic and dignified, take the first candidate merely occupying a hylegiacal house (flagged in reason as a documented fallback). If not even that exists, hyleg is null.
  8. Derive the alcocoden. Compute almuten(hylegLon, isDay) — the planet with the highest sum of positive essential dignities at the hyleg's degree. That planet is the alcocoden.
  9. Look up the years. Return its three traditional spans from PLANETARY_YEARS (greatest / mean / least). The angular⇒greatest, succedent⇒mean, cadent⇒least choice is left to the caller; all three are returned.

Worked example

Take a diurnal chart. In sect order the first candidate is the Sun; suppose it sits at 5° Leo (longitude 125°).

  • House: say the cusps place 5° Leo in the 11th house → 11 is in {1,7,9,10,11}, so hylegiacal = true.
  • Dignity: the Sun rules Leo by domicile, so essentialDignity('Sun', 125, true) has a row with score > 0 → dignified = true.
  • Strict choice: the Sun is the first candidate that is both aphetic and dignified → hyleg = Sun at 125°.

Now the alcocoden: evaluate almuten(125, true). At 5° Leo the candidate dignities sum roughly as:

The life trajectory

The life trajectory is a single composed reading that walks one natal chart across the whole arc of a life. It does not invent a new technique: it runs the engines already built — essential and accidental dignity, the almuten of the Ascendant, the Lord of the Geniture, sect and temperament, the Hyleg/Alcocoden length-of-life judgement, the year-by-year annual profections, the primary directions to the angles, and the current solar return — then layers a Picatrix overlay (the native's ruling planets, the magical works naturally suited to them, what the Lord of the Year favours, the best/worst operations for the present moment, and a recommended talisman). It answers: read as one historical narrative, what does this nativity say about the shape of a life year by year? Presented as historical practice only; astrology has no demonstrated predictive validity.

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. Set the frame. Read opts.currentDate (default now), opts.years (default 90), house system (default regiomontanus), and nowPlace (defaults to the birth lat/lon). Compute ageNow = ageBetween(birth, currentDate) — whole completed UTC years, clamped to 0, subtracting one if the birthday/time has not yet arrived.
  2. Natal signatures. Take the almuten of the Ascendant (chief dispositor of the Asc degree by essential dignity); the Lord of the Geniture = the one of the seven planets with the greatest total (essential + accidental) dignity, scored over all seven, ties broken by Chaldean order; the sect light = Sun if isDay else Moon.
  3. Temperament (coarse). Look at the signs of the Ascendant, the Moon, and the sect light. Each sign is hot|cold and dry|moist by triplicity (Fire = hot+dry, Air = hot+moist, Earth = cold+dry, Water = cold+moist). Count: hot if at least 2 of 3 are hot, dry if at least 2 of 3 are dry. Map to Choleric (Hot/Dry), Sanguine (Hot/Moist), Melancholic (Cold/Dry), or Phlegmatic (Cold/Moist).
  4. Length of life. Run hyleg() then alcocoden(); both are wrapped in try/catch so a contested chart records a note instead of throwing.
  5. Timeline of profections. For each age 0 .. min(years, ageNow+12), capped so the list is at most 102 entries (upper = min(maxAge, 101)): profected sign index = (natal Asc sign index + age) mod 12; activated house = (age mod 12) + 1 — the Ascendant advances one whole sign/house per completed year, a full circuit every 12 years. For each of the seven planets (as promissor) to the Asc and MC (significators), take the direct zodiacal arc = norm360(planet.lon − angle.lon) and convert to years with the Naibod key 0.985647°/year (the Sun's mean daily motion ≈ 1°/yr); keep 0 ≤ years ≤ 90, sorted ascending.

  8. Picatrix overlay. Ruling planets = [almuten of Asc, Lord of Geniture, sect light], deduped in order. Affinities = Picatrix operations whose ruler is a ruling planet. The 28 lunar mansions

The 28 lunar mansions ("Mansions of the Moon") divide the ecliptic into 28 equal stations the Moon passes through on its roughly 27-to-28-day circuit. Each mansion is a fixed slice of zodiac longitude carrying a traditional magical "use" (good for journeys, love, healing, destruction, etc.). The question this answers is: which mansion is the Moon (or any longitude) in right now, and what is that mansion good for? This module uses the tropical scheme, fixing Mansion 1 at 0° Aries.

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. Define one mansion's arc as MANSION_ARC = 360 / 28 = 12.857142857° (i.e. 12°51′26″). The full zodiac is split into 28 uniform arcs, not 12.
  2. Build the table: mansion n spans start = (n−1) × 12.857143° up to end = n × 12.857143° in ecliptic longitude [0,360). Mansion 1 = 0°–12.857°, Mansion 2 = 12.857°–25.714°, and so on. The signSpan string (e.g. "0°00′ Aries – 12°51′ Aries") is the same value rounded to the arc-minute for display.
  3. Take the input ecliptic longitude (the Moon's tropical longitude, in degrees).
  4. Normalize it into the range [0,360): norm = ((moonLon % 360) + 360) % 360 (handles negatives and values over 360).
  5. Compute the zero-based index: idx = Math.floor(norm / 12.857143), giving 0…27.
  6. Return LUNAR_MANSIONS[idx]. By construction 0° lands in Mansion 1 (idx 0) and 359.9° lands in Mansion 28 (idx 27). Each record carries name, aliases, indicator (marker star), use (Agrippa Bk II.33), and source.

Worked example

Suppose the Moon is at tropical longitude 200.0° (which is 20°00′ Libra).

  • Normalize: ((200 % 360) + 360) % 360 = 200.0° — already in range.
  • Index: floor(200.0 / 12.857143) = floor(15.555…) = 15.
  • LUNAR_MANSIONS[15] is mansion num 16 (zero-based 15).

The 36 decan faces

Each zodiac sign is split into three 10° slices called faces (or decans), giving 36 in all around the wheel. Every face has a planetary ruler — the weakest of the five essential dignities, worth just +1 point if a planet falls in its own face — and a traditional magical image inherited from Agrippa and the Picatrix. This module answers: "Which face does a given ecliptic longitude fall in, who rules it, and what figure does the old tradition assign to it?"

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. Lay out the 36 faces in zodiac order: for sign index s (0 = Aries) and decan d (0,1,2), the face spans start = s*30 + d*10 degrees up to start + 10. So each face is exactly 10° wide.
  2. Assign each face its ruler from the FACES table in dignities-data.js. That table is built in continuous Chaldean order beginning with Mars at 0° Aries: the cycle [Mars, Sun, Venus, Mercury, Moon, Saturn, Jupiter] stepped once per face — ruler = FACE_ORDER[(s*3 + d) % 7]. Rulers are not recomputed here; they are reused verbatim.
  3. Attach the magical image. The text is paraphrased one sentence per face from Agrippa, Three Books of Occult Philosophy II.37 (Freake 1651), stored in image with a short keywords tag.
  4. Where the Picatrix II.11 tradition diverges, store that variant separately in imageAlt (e.g. Cancer face 1, Scorpio face 1). The divergence is not silently resolved; the source field then reads "Agrippa, Three Books II.37; Picatrix II.11" instead of Agrippa alone.
  5. To look up a longitude, normalise it to 0–360 with ((lon % 360) + 360) % 360, then index DECAN_FACES[Math.floor(norm / 10)]. Dividing by 10 and flooring picks the face directly — no orbs or thresholds are involved.

Worked example

Take a planet at 15° Leo. Leo is sign index s = 4, so its absolute ecliptic longitude is 4*30 + 15 = 135°.

  • Normalise: ((135 % 360) + 360) % 360 = 135.
  • Pick the face: Math.floor(135 / 10) = 13 → DECAN_FACES[13], the second face of Leo (decan 2, spanning 130°–140°, i.e. 10°–20° Leo).
  • Ruler: FACE_ORDER[(4*3 + 1) % 7] = FACE_ORDER[13 % 7] = FACE_ORDER[6] = Jupiter.

The 15 Behenian fixed stars & precession

The Behenian stars are a canon of fifteen powerful fixed stars from Hermes (via Agrippa) used in talismanic magic, each with its own planetary nature, gemstone, plant, and magical "use." Because fixed stars drift along the ecliptic by precession (~50.29″ per year, about 1° every 72 years), a catalogue longitude is only valid for one epoch. This explainer answers: where does each Behenian star actually fall on your chart's date, and which of your planets sit conjunct one?

How it's calculated, step by step

  1. Start from the catalogue longitude lon2020, stored in ecliptic degrees 0..360 (0 = 0°Aries, 30 = 0°Taurus, ...). These are the star positions at the epoch BEHENIAN_EPOCH = 2020. The canonical 15th star is Alkaid (177.2°); Fomalhaut is flagged modern: true and is excluded from chart scans.
  2. Convert the chart Date to a fractional year: yearOf = UTC year + UTC month / 12 (month/12 is precise enough for a ~50″/yr drift).
  3. Compute the precessed longitude: behenianLongitude = norm360( lon2020 + PRECESSION_DEG_PER_YEAR × (yearOf − 2020) ), where PRECESSION_DEG_PER_YEAR = 50.29 / 3600 = 0.0139694°/yr and norm360(x) = ((x % 360) + 360) % 360 wraps to 0..360.
  4. To find hits, loop over every non-modern Behenian star and every chart planet with a numeric lon. Compute the smallest circular separation: d = |norm360(planetLon) − norm360(starLon)|; sep = d > 180 ? 360 − d : d.
  5. If sep ≤ orb (default conjunction orb orb = 6°), record a hit { star, planet, sep, lon }. Only conjunctions are tested — no other aspects.

Worked example

Take Spica (lon2020 = 204.1, nature Venus + Mercury) for a chart dated 2026-06-25, against a Sun at 205.0° (25°Lib00′).

  • Fractional year: 2026 + 5/12 = 2026.4167 (June is UTC month index 5).
  • Years since epoch: 2026.4167 − 2020 = 6.4167.
  • Precession applied: 0.0139694 × 6.4167 = 0.0896°.
  • Precessed longitude: 204.1 + 0.0896 = 204.1896° → about 24°11.4′ Libra.
  • Separation from Sun: |205.0 − 204.1896| = 0.81°, which is ≤ 180, so sep = 0.81°.
  • Test: 0.81 ≤ 6 → hit. Result: { star: 'Spica', planet: 'Sun', sep: 0.81, lon: 204.19 } — Sun conjunct Spica (riches, favour, wins lawsuits).

Note: without precession the star would read at its 2020 longitude (204.1°); over ~6 years the 0.09° shift is tiny, but over a century it is ~1.4° — enough to move a star in or out of orb, which is why the code never treats one epoch as "now."

How it's calculated, step by step

The wings — cross-tradition atlas

The Great Confluence — the era-warped atlas & its transmission map

The Great Confluence plots nine wisdom traditions as parallel lanes on one vertical time axis and draws, as directed arcs, the documented transmissions between them — a book put into a new language, a commentary, an answer, a fusion. It maps influence, never validity: an arc says the Picatrix reached Latin, not that its talismans work; that the Upaniṣads reached Persian, not that Vedānta is physics. Two honest problems have visible, disclosed solutions: how to draw 3,500 years when most entries fall after 1500 CE, and how to keep fabricated "journeys" from ever looking like documented ones.

The era-warped scale (why the timeline stretches, and how it says so)

A linear axis makes modernity an unreadable smear; a logarithmic one lies quietly. The atlas instead uses a piecewise-linear, era-warped scale: within each era band the mapping year → pixel is exactly linear (local distances are truthful), and the pixels-per-century constant steps up at explicit era boundaries chosen where history hinges and the data thins — not to equalise band heights. The warp is disclosed, never hidden: every band prints its own scale on the map, one century-bracket samples that scale physically in the ruler gutter, and the tick spacing visibly widens down the page. It is the same covenant as the Julian/Gregorian double-dates elsewhere on the site.

The layout algorithm

The nine lanes sit in a fixed left-to-right order chosen once, at design time, to minimise expected arc crossings — the two historically heaviest bridges (Sufi Islam and Yoga & Vedānta) flank a neutral central "confluence" spine, so most long arcs travel through the middle, which is exactly the story. Every node’s vertical position is timeScale(sortYear) and is never moved in time; where several entries in one lane crowd the same instant, a deterministic greedy pass stacks them onto sub-track rows (centre first, then outward), each row an offset column within the lane, so two marks on one row can never overlap. Transmission edges are drawn as cubic Béziers with vertical tangents — each arc leaves its source downward and arrives at its (later) target downward, so every journey visibly flows with time. Uncertainty is drawn as uncertainty: a decade or century of scholarly estimate becomes a soft band behind the mark, a composition-range becomes a capsule, and a ⚑ flags a point where the sources disagree.

The data & its provenance

The atlas’s 190 entries and 155 transmission edges were compiled from nine independent research domains (kabbalah, yoga-vedānta, tantra-rasaśāstra, Buddhist, Daoist, Western alchemy/Hermetica, Abrahamic mysticism, and two cross-tradition "confluence" sets), each adversarially verified — a pass that logged 109 corrections across the domains before merge. Every body, source and contested block is reproduced verbatim; four synthesis entries (Jung, the Corpus Hermeticum, the Picatrix, the Yijing) were authored to resolve cross-domain references and are cited to the same standard. R32 adds two modern yoga-vedānta figures — Swami Vivekananda (1863–1902) and Paramahansa Yogananda (1893–1952) — with four documented edges tying them to the 1893 Chicago Parliament, Raja Yoga (1896), the Yoga Sūtras, and Autobiography of a Yogi (1946). Each entry carries an epistemic label — documented / disputed / debunked / conspiracy — and debunked and conspiracy material stays on the map, drawn faint, because the record of what people falsely believed is also history. Where sources disagree, both positions are shown with their citations and never resolved — the site’s standing rule.

The edges are graded too — the label grades the claim, not the doctrine

Every one of the 155 transmission arcs now carries its own epistemic label, grading the claim of transmission itself — that a book was demonstrably read, rendered, answered or absorbed — not the doctrine it carried. Of the 155, 145 are documented, 9 disputed, and 1 debunked. A disputed arc is drawn faded with a ⚑ at its midpoint; the single debunked arc — the Kybalion’s claim of an ancient Hermetic source, which does not exist (Deslippe) — is drawn faint and struck, never removed. Each arc also stores the single best witness for its claim, shown on the hover card and in every entry’s Journeys list. So a fabricated journey is legible as fabricated: the same honesty covenant the nodes obey, now applied to the lines between them.
