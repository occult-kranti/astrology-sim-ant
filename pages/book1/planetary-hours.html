Planetary Hours

The day is divided into twelve equal “hours” between sunrise and sunset, and the night into twelve between sunset and the next sunrise — so the hours are unequal, longer by day in summer. Each is ruled by a planet in the Chaldean order, beginning at sunrise with the ruler of the weekday.

How it works

The Chaldean order arranges the planets from slowest to fastest: Saturn ♄ · Jupiter ♃ · Mars ♂ · Sun ☉ · Venus ♀ · Mercury ☿ · Moon ☽. The planetary day begins not at midnight but at sunrise; the first hour is ruled by the planet of the weekday (Sun→Sunday, Moon→Monday, Mars→Tuesday, Mercury→Wednesday, Jupiter→Thursday, Venus→Friday, Saturn→Saturday). Each following hour steps one place along the Chaldean order.

Why the days are named as they are Because 24 hours leave a remainder of three when divided among the seven planets, each day's first hour falls three places further along the Chaldean ring — which is exactly the order of the weekdays. The same Chaldean order also rules the faces (decans) of the zodiac.

Sunrise/sunset are computed astronomically for the place and date. In horary, Lilly checks whether the planetary hour-ruler agrees with the ruler of the Ascendant as a test of the figure's radicality — see the Horary Calculator.
