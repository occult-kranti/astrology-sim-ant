Jung & astrology — the psychologist among the horoscopes

In 1911 C. G. Jung told Freud that his evenings were "taken up very largely with astrology" — that he was making horoscopic calculations "in order to find a clue to the core of psychological truth." For the next fifty years astrology ran through his work: he cast charts for patients in difficult diagnoses, read the zodiac as the projected psychology of antiquity, hung the argument of Aion on the precession of the equinoxes, and staked his theory of synchronicity on a statistical marriage experiment with 483 couples' horoscopes — an experiment that failed, as he himself admitted, and which taught him something stranger than astrology instead.

Honest note — please read Jung used astrology; he did not validate it. His considered position was that astrology works — when it seems to — as projected psychology and synchronicity, not as a causal science; his one formal test found nothing beyond chance, and he said so in print. This section reconstructs what he actually did, wrote and calculated, with citations throughout — the history of ideas, described never prescribed.

Why this belongs on this site

The Workbench computes the astrology of Lilly and the Picatrix as history made calculable. Jung is the modern hinge of that history: the man who moved astrology's claims from the sky into the psyche — where they could be studied as meaning — and who then tested the sky-claim empirically and watched it dissolve. His story is the site's honest framing, lived: the mathematics is real, the correspondences are human, and the one formal experiment came out null. See Sources & Science.
