Sources & reading — where the astrology is in Jung

Every claim on these pages is drawn from Jung's own texts or from the scholarship on them. This is the map: the primary Collected Works (with exactly where astrology appears in each), the key dated letters, and the secondary literature — including the indispensable one-volume collection of all Jung's astrology writing.

A shortcut If you read one book: Safron Rossi & Keiron Le Grice (eds.), Jung on Astrology (Routledge, 2017) — it gathers every passage on astrology from across the Collected Works, the seminars and the letters into a single annotated volume. For the critical scholarship: Maggie Hyde, Jung and Astrology (1992).

Primary — the Collected Works & other writings

WorkDateWhere the astrology is
Psychological Types (CW 6)1921 Astrology among the ancient typologies; the four functions of consciousness that later Jungians map to the elements.
The Structure and Dynamics of the Psyche (CW 8)1952/1960 "Synchronicity: An Acausal Connecting Principle" — the theory, and "An Astrological Experiment" (the 483-couple marriage study), the single most important astrological text in the corpus.
Aion: Researches into the Phenomenology of the Self (CW 9ii)1951 Ch. VI, "The Sign of the Fishes" — the precession of the equinoxes, the Piscean aeon, Christ and the two fishes, the coming of Aquarius.
Civilization in Transition (CW 10)1958 "Flying Saucers: A Modern Myth" — the changing aeon and the constellation of a new God-image; astrological framing of a modern phenomenon.
Alchemical Studies (CW 13)1967 "The Spirit Mercurius" — the archetype behind the planet Mercury, the trickster-psychopomp of the work.
Mysterium Coniunctionis (CW 14)1955–56 The Sol–Luna coniunctio — the alchemical marriage of Sun and Moon that underlies the chart-reading of the luminaries and the marriage experiment's key aspect.
The Spirit in Man, Art, and Literature (CW 15)1930 "Richard Wilhelm: In Memoriam" — "astrology represents the summation of all the psychological knowledge of antiquity."
The ETH Lectures (Philemon Series)1933–41 Jung's lectures at the ETH Zürich, where he expounds the horoscope and the archetypal reading of the planets to his students.
The Red Book (Liber Novus)1914–30 / 2009 The visionary source-work; Liz Greene's studies trace its astrological substructure (see below).
Memories, Dreams, Reflections1962 The autobiography (with Aniela Jaffé); the temperament and inner life the horoscope was read against.

The key letters

  • To Sigmund Freud, 1911 — "my evenings are taken up very largely with astrology… I make horoscopic calculations in order to find a clue to the core of psychological truth." (The Freud/Jung Letters, ed. McGuire.)
  • To B. V. Raman, 6 September 1947 — that in difficult diagnoses he "usually" gets a horoscope "for a further point of view from an entirely different angle," and that the data has often clarified a puzzling case. (C. G. Jung Letters, vol. 1.)
  • To André Barbault, 26 May 1954 — the horoscope "corresponds to a definite moment in the colloquy of the gods, that is to say the psychic archetypes." (Letters, vol. 2.)
  • The Pauli letters, 1932–1958 — the long exchange with the physicist Wolfgang Pauli on synchronicity, the experiment, and the archetypal ground of physics. (Atom and Archetype: The Pauli/Jung Letters, ed. Meier.)
  • The Markus Fierz correspondence — the physicist's statistical correction of the marriage experiment, which Jung accepted. (Discussed in Main and in the CW 8 apparatus.)

Scholarship & the post-Jungian legacy

  • Gret Baumann-Jung, "Some Reflections on the Horoscope of C. G. Jung," Spring (1975) — Jung's astrologer daughter on her father's own nativity; the source of the birth data this site uses.
  • Safron Rossi & Keiron Le Grice (eds.), Jung on Astrology (Routledge, 2017) — the collected astrology texts in one volume, with a substantial introduction.
  • Maggie Hyde, Jung and Astrology (Aquarian/HarperCollins, 1992) — the standard critical study, sharp on synchronicity, projection, and the experiment.
  • Liz Greene, Jung's Studies in Astrology and The Astrological World of Jung's Liber Novus (Routledge, 2018) — the deep archival account of Jung's own astrological practice and the Red Book's chart.
  • Roderick Main, Jung on Synchronicity and the Paranormal (1997) and Revelations of Chance (2007) — the philosophy of synchronicity and the experiment's logic.
  • Dane Rudhyar, The Astrology of Personality (1936) — the first thoroughgoing fusion of Jungian psychology with astrology, and the fountainhead of "psychological astrology."
  • James Hillman — the senex/puer archetypes and the archetypal-psychology reading of the planetary gods.
  • Richard Tarnas, Cosmos and Psyche (2006) — the most ambitious modern extension of the archetypal, Jungian view of astrology (and correspondingly contested).
