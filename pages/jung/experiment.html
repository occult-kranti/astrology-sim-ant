The astrological marriage experiment — and its honest re-run

For the Synchronicity essay (1952) Jung mounted the one formal empirical test of astrology he ever ran: collect the horoscopes of hundreds of married couples, tally the cross-aspects between the spouses' charts, and ask whether the tradition's "marriage aspects" — above all the Sun–Moon conjunction, astrology's own image of the marital coniunctio — occur more often between real spouses than chance allows. The three classic aspects did top his three batches in turn. The statistics, honestly done, showed nothing.

The honest headline Jung's best batch rate was about 10%. At his own 8° orb, chance pairing alone yields ≈ 9% — as the simulation below shows live, and as the physicist Markus Fierz's correction showed him. Jung conceded the point in print, and reinterpreted the run of lucky aspects as synchronicity — a meaningful coincidence involving the experimenters' own hopeful psyche — not as evidence that the stars arrange marriages.

What Jung took from the failure

The experiment was designed to illustrate synchronicity, and its failure became its lesson. That the traditional marriage aspects — the Sun–Moon coniunctio above all — surfaced at the top of each successive batch struck Jung not as astral causation (the numbers forbade that) but as a meaningful coincidence keyed to the psychic situation of the experimenters themselves: a "secret mutual connivance," the archetype of the marriage-union constellating in the material just as the researchers hoped to find it. Whatever one makes of that reading, the empirical verdict he accepted stands: the couples' charts held nothing that chance does not hold.
