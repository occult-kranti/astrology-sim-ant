U1The nine grahas

Jyotiṣa begins with the nine grahas ("seizers"): the Sun (Sūrya) and Moon (Candra) — the two luminaries — Mars (Maṅgala), Mercury (Budha), Jupiter (Guru), Venus (Śukra) and Saturn (Śani), plus the two lunar nodes Rāhu and Ketu, which are computed shadow-points, not bodies. Each graha carries a bundle of attributed characters — a nature (naisargika benefic or malefic), a "caste" (varṇa), an element and humour (tridoṣa), a gender, and a long list of correspondences (a colour, a metal, a gemstone, a day, a deity, a direction). The Sun, Mars and Saturn are counted natural malefics; Jupiter, Venus and a waxing Moon natural benefics; Mercury takes the colour of whatever it joins. CONTESTED exactly when the Moon ceases to be benefic is stated differently across texts (a bright-fortnight rule vs a "within so-many degrees of the Sun" rule). The grahas' other role is as kārakas — significators: the Sun is the kāraka of the soul and the father, the Moon of the mind and the mother, Jupiter of children and wisdom, Venus of the spouse, and so on.

Classical sources
  • Graha characters, natures, castes, colours, metals, deities — BPHS ch. 3 "Graha Characters and Description" (Santhanam trans. 1984); Bṛhat Jātaka ch. 2 (Varāhamihira).
  • Natural benefic / malefic, incl. the conditional Moon and Mercury — BPHS 3.24–25; Bṛhat Jātaka 2.5.
  • Kārakatvas (significations) — BPHS ch. 32; the maximalist lists of Uttara Kālāmṛta ch. 5 (cited as the fullest witness, not the shortest).
Honest frame The graha–colour–metal–gem–deity correspondences are cultural attributions with no physical mechanism. They are recorded here as the tradition's symbolic map — not as a recommendation to wear, avoid or acquire anything. This page builds no "your gemstone" tool and endorses none; the correspondence tables are history.

U2The twelve rāśis

The zodiac is divided into twelve rāśis (signs), each ruled by a graha and each carrying a grid of classifications the tradition reads before anything else: odd / even (male / female), movable / fixed / dual (cara / sthira / dvisvabhāva), an element (fire / earth / air / water), a "caste", a body-type, and a set of rising characters — divā-bali / rātri-bali (day- or night-strong), śīrṣodaya / pṛṣṭhodaya / ubhayodaya (head-rising, back-rising, or both), and dvipada / catuṣpada (two- or four-footed). A whole block of the course then reads the Sun through each of the twelve signs. One caution belongs here more than anywhere: the sign a person calls their "star sign" is the tropical Sun sign; Jyotiṣa uses the sidereal zodiac, so the sidereal Sun sign, the Moon sign (rāśi) and the rising sign (lagna) are usually three different signs. The ~24° gap is the ayanāṁśa — the one place this page touches Western astrology.

Classical sources
  • The twelve rāśis & their lords — BPHS ch. 4 "Zodiacal Rasis Described".
  • The classification axes (bali, udaya, pada-count, element, "caste", benefic/malefic signs) — BPHS 4.4–30; Bṛhat Jātaka 1.10–20; Sārāvalī ch. 3.
  • The Sun (and planets) through the signs — Bṛhat Jātaka ch. 20; Sārāvalī chs. 21–29.
  • Lagna vs Moon-sign vs Sun-sign — BPHS ch. 5; the sidereal ↔ tropical shift is the ayanāṁśa arithmetic.
Honest frame The old varṇa ("caste") labels on the signs are medieval social-history language, kept in quotation marks and reported, not endorsed. And "which sign am I?" has no single answer: tropical Sun ≠ sidereal Sun ≠ Moon sign ≠ lagna — the site shows all of them and the arithmetic that separates them, so nothing is smuggled in as "your sign".

U3The chart — bhāvas & the two formats

The twelve bhāvas (houses) carry the life-areas: the 1st (tanu — body, self), 2nd (dhana — wealth, speech), 3rd (sahaja — siblings, courage), 4th (sukha — home, mother), and on to the 12th (vyaya — loss, liberation). Jyotiṣa draws the same chart in two regional formats: the North Indian diamond, where the houses are fixed on the page (the 1st always the top-centre diamond) and the sign-numbers move, and the South Indian square, where the signs are fixed in a 4×4 grid and the lagna is marked with a stroke. They encode identical information; only the drawing convention differs. The course then teaches the single most-used sentence in the whole tradition: "the lord of house X (its bhāveśa) sits in house Y" — reading each house through the placement of the graha that rules its sign.

Classical sources
  • The bhāvas & their significations — BPHS ch. 11 (intro) + chs. 12–23 (Tanu → Vyaya, one per bhāva); Phaladīpikā 1.17–19.
  • The bhāveśa doctrine ("lord of X in Y", 144 combinations) — BPHS ch. 24 "Effects of the Bhava Lords"; Phaladīpikā ch. 20.
  • North vs South chart formats — a regional scribal convention, not a śāstra doctrine; cited to modern witnesses (B.V. Raman; the Jagannath Hora documentation), not to a classical verse.
Honest frame The North / South split is a diagram convention, like drawing a graph with the origin at a different corner — it changes nothing about the chart. Where the classical texts do speak (the bhāva significations, the bhāveśa effects) the claims are doctrinal and unvalidated; the drawing formats are simply how different regions ink the paper.

U4Graha strength (elementary)

Before any judgement, the tradition weighs each graha. The first, coarsest measure is by sign dignity: exalted (uccha), in its own sign (svakṣetra), in its mūlatrikoṇa, or debilitated (nīca). Then three modifiers: combustion (asta) — a graha too near the Sun is held "burnt" and weakened; retrogression (vakra) — read as a kind of strength (ceṣṭā-bala); and friendship — the naisargika (permanent) friendships of BPHS 3.55–57, compounded with the tātkālika (temporary, by relative house position) into the five-fold pañcadhā maitrī matrix (great friend … great enemy). The mature form of all this is ṣaḍbala, the six-fold strength — the "grown-up version" the elementary course points toward. CONTESTED the exact combustion arcs differ between the Sūrya-Siddhānta and the Phaladīpikā line (and again for retrograde planets); the site carries the classical set with the dispute flagged.

Classical sources
  • Exaltation / debilitation / own / mūlatrikoṇa — BPHS 3.49–54.
  • Combustion (asta) arcs — Sūrya-Siddhānta 9.6–9; Phaladīpikā ch. 8/9 (Moon 12°, Mars 17°, Mercury 14°/12° retro, Jupiter 11°, Venus 10°/9° retro, Saturn 15°) — contested between the sources.
  • Retrogression as ceṣṭā strength — BPHS ch. 27.
  • Naisargika + tātkālika + pañcadhā (compound) maitrī — BPHS 3.55–60.
Honest frame A source-fidelity note: in the teaching playlist that fixes this page's order, session 32 is unconfirmed and session 33 is missing, so the compound-friendship lesson is a gap in that witness. The pañcadhā-maitrī doctrine is included here regardless, straight from BPHS 3.58–60 — the citation, not the video, is the source.

U5Grahas in the bhāvas

The largest single block of the course — some eighty lessons — is the graha-in-bhāva delineation literature: what each planet is held to signify in each of the twelve houses, house by house, planet by planet. This is formulaic effect-literature, and its most honest feature is that the classical texts contradict each other — the eleventh-house Sun that Phaladīpikā reads as a sorrowless king, Sārāvalī sours into an affectionless servant. The site's delineation browser holds 108 cited records with the two witnesses side by side and never merged, so the disagreement is shown as a feature, not smoothed into one "meaning".

Classical sources
  • Mantreśvara, Phaladīpikā ch. 8 (S.S. Sareen trans.) — the backbone, all nine grahas, verse-checkable.
  • Kalyāṇavarman, Sārāvalī ch. 30 (R. Santhanam 1983) — the second witness, Sun through Saturn only. Sārāvalī does not treat the nodes in houses, so Rāhu / Ketu are single-witness (Phaladīpikā 8.25–33 only), carried with the absence noted honestly.
Honest frame This is the most "horoscope-like" material on the whole site, and the defence is deliberate: per-record citations and the never-merged two-witness display. The harsh, deterministic judgments are reproduced unsoftened for fidelity — they describe nothing about any actual person. Note too that the teaching playlist's coverage of the 2nd and 12th houses is thin — a real gap in that witness, stated rather than papered over.

U6House classifications

The houses fall into classes that drive the tradition's functional judgement. The kendras (angles: 1,4,7,10) and trikoṇas (trines: 1,5,9) are the strong, auspicious houses; the trika or duḥsthāna (6,8,12) the difficult ones; the triṣaḍāya (3,6,11) and upachaya (3,6,10,11) growth-houses. From these come the functional labels: a yogakāraka — a single graha that rules both a kendra and a trikoṇa for a given lagna (Mars for Cancer & Leo, Venus for Capricorn & Aquarius, Saturn for Taurus & Libra) — and, conversely, kendrādhipati doṣa, in which a natural benefic owning a kendra is held to lose its beneficence. The tradition also reads graha dṛṣṭi (planetary aspects: all grahas the 7th; Mars also the 4th & 8th, Jupiter the 5th & 9th, Saturn the 3rd & 10th), and names certain grahas māraka ("killer": the lords and occupants of the 2nd and 7th) in its length-of-life doctrine. Two live disputes: CONTESTED kendrādhipati doṣa (Laghu Parāśarī affirms it; Uttara Kālāmṛta limits it) and CONTESTED whether dṛṣṭi is read whole-sign (popular teaching) or degree-graded (the sphuṭa scheme of BPHS ch. 26) — both shown both ways.

Classical sources
  • Kendra / trikoṇa / trika / triṣaḍāya / upachaya, and yogakāraka per lagna — BPHS ch. 34 "Yogakarakas" (34.13–20); Laghu Parāśarī 6–13.
  • Graha dṛṣṭi — BPHS ch. 26 (both the special aspects and the degree-graded virūpa scheme).
  • Māraka grahas — BPHS ch. 44 "Maraka Planets".
  • Kendrādhipati doṣa — Laghu Parāśarī 6–7; the counter-position in Uttara Kālāmṛta (contested).
Honest frame — read this one carefully Māraka is the tradition's death-timing doctrine, and nothing on this site predicts anything. The tools compute only the classification — which grahas the doctrine labels māraka — never a date, a span, or a "danger period", exactly as the site already handles the Western length-of-life doctrines (hyleg / alcocoden). It is displayed as history, with the strongest possible non-predictive caveat.

U7Yogas & synthesis

A yoga is a named planetary combination held to carry a specific effect. The course teaches the canon a beginner meets first: Budha-Āditya (Sun–Mercury together — the name is modern, the effect old); Gaja-Kesarī (Jupiter in a kendra from the Moon); Candra-Maṅgala; Kemadruma (an isolated Moon) with its cancellations; the Dhana (wealth) combinations; the Rājayogas (a lord of a kendra joined to a lord of a trikoṇa); the Vipreet Rājayoga trio — Harṣa / Sarala / Vimala (the 6th / 8th / 12th lords in the trika houses); and Nīca-bhaṅga Rājayoga (a "cancelled debilitation"). Two of these are genuinely disputed and shown both ways: CONTESTED Gaja-Kesarī — the bare rule (Phaladīpikā 6.14) versus the strength-conditioned rule (Jātaka Pārijāta 7.116), a dispute the teacher's own session 124 engages — and CONTESTED Nīca-bhaṅga, whose cancellation conditions differ across three texts (never reduced to a boolean, but reported as a labelled condition list). The unit closes with the modern step-by-step chart-analysis method (lagna & lord → Moon & nakṣatra → dignities → bhāveśa grid → classifications → yogas → daśā).

Classical sources
  • Budha-Āditya, Candra-Maṅgala & conjunction yogas — BPHS ch. 36; Sārāvalī chs. 13–17 (the Budha-Āditya name is late).
  • Gaja-Kesarī — bare rule Phaladīpikā 6.14; conditioned rule Jātaka Pārijāta 7.116 (contested).
  • Dhana yogas — BPHS chs. 41–42; Rājayogas — BPHS chs. 39–40 + Laghu Parāśarī.
  • Vipreet Rājayoga (Harṣa / Sarala / Vimala) — Phaladīpikā 6.63 / 6.65 / 6.69 (Sareen/Sastri numbering); general principle Uttara Kālāmṛta 4.22.
  • Nīca-bhaṅga condition sets — Phaladīpikā, Jātaka Pārijāta and BPHS variants (contested; encoded as a labelled list, never a boolean).
  • The walkthrough method — no single classical locus; a modern synthesis (B.V. Raman, How to Judge a Horoscope) cited as a modern witness.
Honest frame Even the tradition's own teachers dispute whether some of these yogas "work" — session 124 of the source playlist mounts an effectiveness critique of Gaja-Kesarī that mirrors Jātaka Pārijāta's extra conditions. A contested rule is shown as contested, both ways, and the detector never collapses it to a single yes/no.

U8Where this course stops

An honest handoff. Verified across all 132 lesson titles, the teaching playlist that orders this page never reaches several of the most important Parāśarī systems: the daśās (the vimśottarī timing spine), the nakṣatras beyond passing mention, the divisional charts (vargas), ṣaḍbala and aṣṭakavarga as systems, transits (gocara), praśna, muhūrta, and remedies-as-a-unit. That is where an introductory course honestly ends — and where this site's computing engines pick up. The Jagannath Hora reading already computes the vimśottarī daśā, the sixteen vargas, the aṣṭakavarga (SAV), a partial ṣaḍbala and the full pañcāṅga for any chart; the wider Jyotiṣa apparatus has its own tools.

Where the classical texts continue
  • Vimśottarī daśā — BPHS chs. 46–48. Ṣoḍaśavarga — BPHS chs. 6–7. Aṣṭakavarga — BPHS chs. 66–67. Ṣaḍbala — BPHS ch. 27. Avasthās — BPHS ch. 45. Upagrahas — BPHS ch. 3.61–70 & ch. 25.
