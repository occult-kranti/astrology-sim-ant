The editions & the licensing

Every segment in this wing is built from public-domain or CC0 sources, cited per record, with the licence of each layer stamped on every line. This page sets out the edition stack, states plainly what may be quoted verbatim and what is cite-only, documents the gloss discipline and the refrain model, and admits the wing's coverage limits. The licensing situation here is unusually good — a modern, accurate, zero-restriction translation layer — and this page shows why.

SuttaCentral's translations — CC0 (public-domain dedication)

The wing's primary translation layer for the Pāli texts is Bhikkhu Sujato's, dedicated to the public domain. Doubly sourced — the site licensing page and the data repository — quoted verbatim:

"All original material created by SuttaCentral is dedicated to the Public Domain by means of Creative Commons Zero (CC0 1.0 Universal)." — suttacentral.net/licensing
"All translations created in Bilara and supported by SuttaCentral are dedicated to the Public Domain by means of the Creative Commons Public Domain (CC0) license." — suttacentral/bilara-data, LICENSE.md (branch published)

Note honestly: the legacy translations hosted on SuttaCentral (Bodhi, Ñāṇamoli, Horner, Thanissaro and others originally published elsewhere) are not CC0 — they keep their own terms and are never ingested here. Only Sujato's own SuttaCentral translations are reproduced.

The root Pāli — Mahāsaṅgīti Tipiṭaka (declared public domain)

SuttaCentral distributes the root Pāli text — the Mahāsaṅgīti Tipiṭaka Buddhavasse 2500 (a corrected electronic descendant of the 1956 Chaṭṭha Saṅgāyana edition) — as public domain:

"The original texts of Buddhism in Pali, Chinese, Sanskrit, Tibetan, and other languages are in the public domain. Such material does not fall within the scope of copyright." — suttacentral.net/licensing

The underlying canonical text is ancient and uncopyrightable; the edition is distributed as PD by its publisher-partner. Any residual edition-copyright risk is negligible and mitigated by that explicit declaration — stated here rather than hidden.

SuttaCentral's request — acknowledged

SuttaCentral asks that its content not be scraped for AI datasets:

"SuttaCentral does not make use of artifically-generated data. We politely request that our content not be scraped or used in any way for the creation of datasets for generative AI or similar." — suttacentral.net/licensing

CC0 makes this a request, not a licence term. This wing is a hand-curated, attributed, human-verified study edition — not an AI training dataset. Every text was encoded from the cited editions by hand-driven scripts against the segment-aligned source, and this page carries the prominent attribution and linkback that is the site's answer to the request. If you value this text, read and support it at SuttaCentral.

The gloss discipline — the licence firewall

The word-by-word gloss prose is written originally for this wing. Grammatical facts — case, number, root, compound resolution — are uncopyrightable; expressive definition prose is not. So:

  • Glosses are original prose, informed by and cited to the public-domain PED (1921–25) for Pāli and Monier-Williams for the Heart Sūtra's Sanskrit — cited per record as PED s.v. <headword> or MW s.v. <headword>.
  • The Digital Pāḷi Dictionary (CC BY-NC-SA 4.0) was used strictly as a lookup / verification instrument to check grammar identifications and sense-choices. No DPD text is reproduced — its NonCommercial-ShareAlike licence is incompatible with this wing's PD/CC0 posture, so it stays a firewall.
  • Each record's licence stamp names the licence of every layer: CC0 for the translation and root Pāli, PD for the older editions, and original for the gloss prose.

The refrain model — the peyyāla

Prose suttas (MN 118 here) repeat fixed blocks after each item, and the manuscript tradition itself abbreviates them with …pe… ("and so on"). Expanding every repetition word-by-word would gloss the same words dozens of times; silently dropping them would misrepresent the text. So each refrain is glossed once, and where it recurs it is shown collapsed with a "show the full refrain" toggle that expands to the full word-by-word — the reader always sees the structure, and nothing is silently collapsed. The manuscripts' own …pe… is the precedent for the device.

Transcription hygiene

  • Verbatim from the source. Each Pāli/Sanskrit line is the Bilara root segment value; each translation is the byte-value of the Sujato segment (the wing tidies only <j> line-join markers and trailing spaces for display — the shipped data keeps them verbatim). A mechanical diff against the source JSON shows zero differences.
  • NFC-normalised, Roman script. The Mahāsaṅgīti is a Roman-script (IAST) edition — there is no Devanāgarī column. Text is Unicode NFC; no Vedic-accent codepoints; no smart-quote mojibake.
  • Reconstruction invariant. Joining a segment's word records with single spaces rebuilds its Pāli/Sanskrit line exactly — the automatic proof that no word was silently skipped in glossing. Fused (sandhi) tokens are one record whose gloss opens with the resolution "= a + b: …".

Coverage & limits

This wing word-by-word's three texts in full — the Metta Sutta (Snp 1.8), the shorter Heart Sūtra, and the Ānāpānassati Sutta (MN 118). That is a deliberate, tiny slice of a canon that runs to roughly forty printed volumes. The wider canon is catalogued, not glossed, with links out to SuttaCentral for the rest. The counts on the wing home are computed live from the data, so any partial state is always truthful. Where a reading is genuinely disputed — the Heart Sūtra's origins (Nattier), the meaning of kāya in the first breath-tetrad — the wing shows both positions and resolves neither.

The rule, again Everything in this wing is a historical religious text, studied closely. The texts make claims — about rebirth, meditative attainments, and liberation — that are reported as what the texts say, described never prescribed, with no demonstrated validity as claims about the world.
