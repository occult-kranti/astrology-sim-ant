The Buddhist scriptures — word by word

A study wing over a curated Buddhist canon, built the way the Yoga Sūtras wing is built: every segment in its original language (Pāli or Sanskrit, in IAST), a word-by-word gloss — hover, tap or keyboard-focus any word for its meaning, grammar and dictionary citation — and the verbatim modern or historical translation beside it, each record stamped with the licence of every layer. The full canon is some forty printed volumes and will never be glossed word by word; the honesty here is in the curation. Three foundational texts are word-by-word'd in full; the rest is catalogued, with links out to where it lives.

Historical scripture · studied closely No demonstrated validity
Honest note — please read This is a wing over historical religious texts, presented for study. The suttas and sūtras below describe a contemplative path and make claims — about rebirth, the brahmavihāras (the "divine abidings"), the fruits of meditation (jhāna, the awakening factors), and liberation (nibbāna). Those claims are the texts' own, described here never prescribed. Nothing on these pages has demonstrated validity as a claim about the world; this is the close reading of what the texts say and how their words work, not an endorsement of their doctrine or an instruction to practise. The three reading rooms (Tier 1 — full word-by-word)

Every word in these three texts is glossed with its grammar and a citation to the public-domain PTS Pali-English Dictionary (1921–25) or, for the Sanskrit Heart Sūtra, to Monier-Williams. The gloss prose is written for this wing; the translations are reproduced verbatim from public-domain / CC0 sources and are never silently altered. The counts below are computed live from the data:

☸Snp 1.8 · Pāli

The Metta Sutta

The Karaṇīyametta Sutta — the beloved discourse on loving-kindness, ten verses. The wing's cheap, luminous opener: every word glossed, Sujato's CC0 translation alongside.

 ✶Prajñāpāramitā · Sanskrit

The Heart Sūtra

The shorter Prajñāpāramitā-hṛdaya — "form is emptiness, emptiness is form." Sanskrit word-by-word (Müller–Nanjio 1884), Müller's 1894 translation, and the ⚑ Nattier origins crux, unresolved.

 ◎MN 118 · Pāli

The Ānāpānassati Sutta

Mindfulness of breathing — the sixteen steps in four tetrads. A prose sutta: its peyyāla refrains are glossed once and shown honestly, expandable on demand. 154 segments.

The wider canon — catalogued, not glossed

The Pāli canon (the Tipiṭaka) alone runs to roughly forty volumes in the PTS edition; the Mahāyāna and Vajrayāna corpora are larger still. This wing does not pretend to render it — it selects a few foundational texts and glosses them properly, and points honestly at the rest:

  • The three piṭakas. The Vinaya (monastic discipline), the Sutta (discourses — the five Nikāyas: Dīgha, Majjhima, Saṁyutta, Aṅguttara, Khuddaka), and the Abhidhamma (systematic analysis). The two Tier-1 suttas here sit in the Khuddaka (Metta) and Majjhima (MN 118) collections.
  • The Mahāyāna sūtras. The Prajñāpāramitā literature (of which the Heart Sūtra is the shortest distillation), the Lotus, the Diamond, the Pure-Land and Avataṁsaka sūtras, and the Chan/Zen classics.
  • Where the rest lives. Bhikkhu Sujato's complete, public-domain (CC0) translations of the four Nikāyas, the Dhammapada and the Suttanipāta are freely readable and downloadable at SuttaCentral, segment-aligned with the root Pāli — the same source this wing reproduces. See the sources & licensing page for the full edition stack.

Why these three, and why so few? Because a page that glossed everything shallowly would be a worse study tool than one that glosses a little completely — and because honest curation is itself part of the honest framing this whole site keeps.
