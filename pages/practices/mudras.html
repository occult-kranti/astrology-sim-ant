The mudrā catalog — the seals, text by text

The whole-body "seals" (mudrā) of haṭha yoga — locks, inversions, tongue and gaze seals, breath-work — as catalogued by the two classical manuals that define them: the Gheraṇḍa Saṁhitā (chapter 3, twenty-five seals) and the Haṭhayogapradīpikā (chapter 3, ten). Nine of the ten HYP names recur in the Gheraṇḍa; the literature routinely conflates the two sets, and this catalog will not — each text's own description is kept as a separate record, joined by a crosswalk.

The standing note — museum record, described never prescribed Every card below is a historical description of what the cited text instructs its own practitioner, of no demonstrated supernatural validity. The purpose lines report only what the tradition claims. Some of these seals are genuinely dangerous as the texts describe them — khecarī instructs progressively cutting the tongue's frenulum over months; vajrolī is a genito-urinary practice; several are held under forced breath-retention. Those records carry a red harm flag, presented as textual history and named as danger. This is not health, medical, or spiritual advice, and nothing here is anything to attempt.

The crosswalk — the seals named in both texts

Nine of the ten Haṭhayogapradīpikā seals share a name with one of the Gheraṇḍa's twenty-five. They are kept as separate per-source records above; this table joins them. Two rows deserve a close look: khecarī (both texts instruct cutting the frenulum — the plan's claim that the Gheraṇḍa omits the cutting is corrected in the data), and vajrolī / vajroṇī, where a single name covers two entirely different practices.
