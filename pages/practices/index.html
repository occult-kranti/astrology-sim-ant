Practices — the museum of methods

The Great Works wing answers "who wrote what?" — author to book to chapter. This wing is its transpose: "what did people actually DO, and which book defines it?" — practice group to practice to the text that fixes it. Every record is a museum description of what a historical text instructs its own practitioner, cited to a real edition — never a manual. The flagship built this round is the mudrā catalog: the Gheraṇḍa Saṁhitā's twenty-five seals and the Haṭhayogapradīpikā's ten, each with a generated schematic figure in the wing's line-art style.

Historical method · described closely No demonstrated validity
The standing note — please read first These are historical religious and esoteric disciplines of no demonstrated supernatural validity. Every description on these pages reports what a cited historical text instructs its own practitioner — as history, never as an instruction to you. The physiological, magical or spiritual benefits named are the texts' own claims, reported and attributed, never endorsed. Several seals the texts describe are genuinely dangerous (frenulum-cutting, breath-retention, extreme postures, genito-urinary practices); those carry an explicit harm flag, presented as textual history and named as danger. Nothing here is health, medical, or spiritual advice: the site describes, it never prescribes. See Sources & Science.

How this wing works

Each mudrā is a museum record, not a recipe. It carries: the seal's name (IAST + Devanāgarī); a source badge and verse-locus pinned to a verifiable public-domain edition; the description — what the text instructs its practitioner, in the historical voice ("the text instructs…"); the claimed purpose, always attributed ("the text claims…"); the text's own cautions; a prominent harm flag where the instruction is genuinely dangerous; crosswalk links to the counterpart record in the other text; and, where the texts diverge, a ⚑ contested panel that states both positions and resolves neither. The schematic figures are generated for this site — stroke-only line-art, deliberately non-instructional — never traced from a photograph of a technique, and never depicting the dangerous specifics (khecarī's cutting is described in words and flagged, and never drawn; vajrolī is not diagrammed at all).

Why this belongs on the Workbench The rest of this site reads astrology and the oracles as history made calculable, and keeps study wings — the Yoga Sūtras, the Buddhist scriptures — beside its engines. The haṭha seals are the bodily discipline that the same Indian world set beside its philosophy; catalogued here as a museum, they can be studied honestly all the way down to the edition on every line.
