The Moment Scanner — every rulebook, one timeline

Several traditions on this site claim to grade the same moment — each by its own, incompatible rulebook. This tool scans a time range once and lays their verdicts side by side: the Lilly/Picatrix election verdict for a chosen aim, the Chaldean planetary hour, the Indian muhūrta of the sunrise-day, the three avoided kālas, and the classical pañcāṅga screens. One sky, several rulebooks — compared, never merged. Watching them agree and disagree along the timeline is the honest lesson.

Honest note — please read Every column of this scan is a historical doctrine of no demonstrated validity. The astronomy underneath (sunrises, planetary positions, day-divisions) is real and reproducible; the gradings laid over it are the traditions' own, reproduced faithfully for study. The columns are separate rulebooks: when two of them mark the same stretch "good" or "avoid", that agreement means nothing more than a coincidence of conventions — it is not corroboration, and disagreement (the usual case) is not a contradiction to be resolved. Described, never prescribed.

Legend — each column is a different tradition

  • Lilly election — the Latin West's electional verdict (green/amber/red, with its additive score) for the chosen aim: planetary day & hour, the ruler's dignities, the Moon's phase, mansion, void-of-course and via combusta, malefics on the angles, Behenian star contacts. Source: Lilly, Christian Astrology (1647); the Picatrix; Agrippa II. Hover a verdict for its leading testimony. Single-system view: the Election engine.
  • Planetary hour — the Chaldean-order hour ruler of the unequal (seasonal) hour, day arc ÷ 12 and night arc ÷ 12 from sunrise/sunset. The oldest layer of Western election: "operate in the planet's own day and hour."
  • Muhūrta — which of the 30 muhūrtas of the Indian sunrise-to-sunrise day the moment falls in, with the tradition's grading (auspicious/mixed/inauspicious), ★ for Abhijit and the Brāhma muhūrta, ⚑ where the sources disagree. Single-system view: the Muhūrta tool.
  • Kāla — whether the moment falls in one of the three avoided spans of the Indian day (Rāhu-kāla, Yamaghaṇṭa, Gulika-kāla — one eighth of the daylight arc each, picked by the weekday). Day arc only: the night shows no kāla by construction.
  • Pañcāṅga screens — the classical quality verdicts on the moment's tithi (T), yoga (Y), karaṇa (K) and nakṣatra (N): Riktā tithis, the nine avoided yogas, Viṣṭi karaṇa, the sevenfold nakṣatra classes. Sidereal (Lahiri), from the same sky as every other column.

These five rulebooks share the astronomy and nothing else — different day-boundaries (sunrise vs midnight), different zodiacs (tropical vs sidereal), different units (unequal hours vs thirtieths of the day). That is why the scanner compares and never merges.

How this works

This tool adds no new data or doctrine. It steps through the chosen range (30-minute strides, capped at one week) and, at each step, asks the engines that already exist on this site — the same code paths their own pages run:

  • One chart per step is cast on the verified astronomical engine (Regiomontanus houses, the election engine's default) and shared: the election verdict reads its dignities, Moon condition and angles; the sidereal pañcāṅga reads only planet longitudes, the ascendant and the date — none of which depend on the house system, so its limbs are identical to a whole-sign cast (asserted in the headless test).
  • The planetary hour is computed once per step and passed into the election engine so it is never recomputed.
  • The muhūrtas and kālas are properties of the sunrise-day, not of the step — they are computed once per sunrise-to-sunrise day and cached; every step inside that day reuses the same 30 slots and 3 kālas, so the scan's boundaries match the Muhūrta page exactly.
  • The pañcāṅga is computed at every step rather than reused hourly: profiling showed the sidereal snapshot costs well under a millisecond, so a full week's scan stays under a second and no boundary blurring is needed. It remains an instantaneous snapshot — limb end-times are not clocked, so a limb can change between steps.
  • The table compresses runs: a 168-hour scan takes 337 steps, but a row is rendered only when something changes — the Lilly verdict, the hour ruler, the muhūrta, the kāla, or a screen verdict. A row's state holds until the next row.

Engines composed: election.js (electionScore), planetary-hours.js (planetaryHour), muhurta.js (muhurtasOf, kalas, panchangaScreens), vedic.js (castVedic), on astro.js (castChart). Each carries its own citations on its own page.
