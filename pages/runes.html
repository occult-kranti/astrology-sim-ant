Elder Futhark Runes — cast & read

The Elder Futhark is the oldest runic alphabet — 24 staves in three groups of eight (the ættir), used to write the Germanic languages roughly c. 150–700 CE. Here the tradition's lot-casting is reproduced for study: scatter the lots in the manner Tacitus described, or draw a single rune or a three-rune spread. Each stave shows its attested medieval rune-poem stanza above its clearly-flagged modern keyword — never blended.

Honest note — please read The Elder Futhark was first of all a writing system. Magical use is attested in inscriptions (charm-words like alu and laukaz), but no ancient or medieval source gives a rune-by-rune divinatory meaning table — the neat "each rune = one meaning" oracle is a 20th-century construction (Ralph Blum, 1982). The three rune poems are attested; the stanza gists here are original paraphrases after Bruce Dickins (1915, public domain), not a copyrighted modern translation. A historical divinatory art of no demonstrated validity. The lots are drawn by your browser's random generator; nothing leaves the page. No blank rune ships here (see the framing note below).

The reading

A mirror, not a verdict The runes offer images to reflect on. This has no demonstrated predictive validity; it is structured reflection over the attested rune-poem material for study. The modern keyword layer is a 20th-century construction, kept clearly apart from the poems.

The 24 staves (the three ættir)

The three-fold division is attested (Vadstena & Grumpan bracteates, c. 500 CE); the ætt names "Freyr's / Hagall's / Týr's" are a later Icelandic tradition. Every Proto-Germanic name is reconstructed (asterisked) — no Elder-period rune name is directly attested. A ⚑ marks a stave with a flagged dispute.

How the cast is computed

  1. The 24 staves are a fixed, cited dataset (core/data/runes-data.js): each with its verified Unicode codepoint, transliteration, ætt, reconstructed name, attested rune-poem stanzas and a flagged modern keyword.
  2. The app scatters the lots with crypto.getRandomValues and draws the required number of distinct staves (1 for a single draw, 3 for the Tacitus cast and the three-rune spread).
  3. The pure engine (core/runes.js — no randomness, no DOM) validates the draws and assembles the reading: the drawn staves, each poem stanza above the modern keyword, the method note, and the framing.
  4. Nothing is predicted. The output reproduces a 20th-century practice over medieval poem material — described, never prescribed.
