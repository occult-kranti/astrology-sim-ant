Muhūrta — the Indian election of the moment

Astronomy — verified Doctrine — historical Predictive validity — none demonstrated

Muhūrta is Indian astrology's electional art — the doctrine of choosing the moment. The day from sunrise to the next sunrise is cut into 30 muhūrtas (15 by day, 15 by night, each ≈48 minutes), each named and graded by the tradition — from inauspicious Rudra at sunrise to Samudra closing the night, with the famous Abhijit straddling local apparent noon and the pre-dawn Brāhma muhūrta of daily practice. Over this clock the pañcāṅga lays its screens: three avoided kālas each day (Rāhu-kāla, Yamaghaṇṭa, Gulika-kāla — one eighth of the daylight arc, picked by the weekday), and the classical quality gradings of the moment's tithi, yoga, karaṇa and nakṣatra.

Honest note — please read Muhūrta is a historical electional doctrine of no demonstrated validity. The arithmetic on this page is real and reproducible — equal divisions of the measured sunrise/sunset arcs on a verified astronomical engine — but the gradings (auspicious, inauspicious, avoid) are the tradition's, reproduced faithfully for study with their sources; they are never a prediction or advice. Where the sources genuinely disagree (two muhūrta qualities, the Abhijit weekday exception, one table's misprint) the dispute is flagged ⚑ with both positions, never silently resolved.

How this works

The sunrise-bounded day & the 30 muhūrtas

The muhūrta day runs sunrise → next sunrise, taken from the same rise/set search the planetary-hours tool uses (sunrise is computed once, never re-derived). The daylight arc (sunrise→sunset) is cut into 15 equal parts and the night arc (sunset→next sunrise) into 15 more — muhūrtas 1–30, each exactly one fifteenth of its arc (≈48 minutes only on a mean 12h/12h day). Abhijit is day-muhūrta 8: its midpoint is always the mid-day point sunrise + D/2 (an algebraic identity — the middle of 15 parts), i.e. local apparent noon ± 24 minutes on the mean day. Brāhma muhūrta is No. 29, from 2 night-muhūrtas before sunrise to 1 before (04:24–05:12 on the mean day). The 30-name list (Rudra → Samudra) is later jyotiṣa tradition, unattributed in the cited sources — it is not the Taittirīya-Brāhmaṇa III.10.1 list, whose fifteen day-names are entirely different; two of the thirty qualities (#16 Girīśa, #22 Agni) are graded oppositely by the two sources and are flagged ⚑ with both positions.

The three kālas

Rāhu-kāla, Yamaghaṇṭa and Gulika-kāla each occupy one eighth of the daylight arc; which eighth depends on the weekday, from three verified octant tables (Rāhu: Sun 8th, Mon 2nd, Tue 7th, Wed 5th, Thu 6th, Fri 4th, Sat 3rd; Yamaghaṇṭa 5/4/3/2/1/7/6; Gulika 7/6/5/4/3/2/1). Gulika's table even has a derivation: assign the day's eight parts lords starting from the weekday's ruler in weekday order (the eighth part lordless) — Gulika is Saturn's part. These are the day tables; nocturnal variants exist and are deliberately not encoded. The tables were verified against two independent published sources and a same-day live pañcāṅga (Ujjain, Monday 2026-07-13: Rāhu 07:32–09:12, Yamaghaṇṭa 10:52–12:32, Gulika 14:12–15:52 — the engine reproduces all three to the minute of its own sunrise).

The vāra correction

The weekday that picks the octant is the sunrise-bounded vāra: the Indian weekday begins at sunrise, not at civil midnight. The site's Vedic pañcāṅga uses the civil-UTC weekday (a documented simplification); this tool computes the vāra itself from the sunrise instant, taking the weekday of the local civil date of that sunrise (local mean time = UTC + longitude/15h — sunrise sits hours from midnight, so time-zone quirks cannot flip the day). When the two disagree, the summary flags it.

The pañcāṅga screens

Four of the five limbs are screened by the classical rules: tithi by the fivefold Nandā/Bhadrā/Jayā/Riktā/Pūrṇā cycle (Riktā — the 4th, 9th and 14th of each paksha — is the avoid class; amāvāsyā is avoided besides); yoga by the nine avoided nitya-yogas (Viṣkambha, Atigaṇḍa, Śūla, Gaṇḍa, Vyāghāta, Vajra, Vyatīpāta, Parigha, Vaidhṛti — the last two mahā-doṣa yogas avoided whole, the rest classically only in their opening ghaṭīs: 3/6/5/6/9/3/–/30/– respectively, 1 ghaṭī = 24 min); karaṇa by Viṣṭi (Bhadrā), the standard avoid, which falls on every 7th half-tithi slot, eight times a lunar month; and nakṣatra by the sevenfold Muhūrta-Cintāmaṇi classification (Dhruva, Cara, Ugra, Miśra, Kṣipra, Mṛdu, Tīkṣṇa — Ugra and Tīkṣṇa avoided for auspicious works). The pañcāṅga here is an instantaneous snapshot — limb end-times are not computed, so opening-ghaṭī rules are stated, not clocked. Every record carries its citation; the classical anchor text is the Muhūrta Cintāmaṇi of Rāma Daivajña (Kāśī, 1600 CE).

A strong, honest caveat None of this predicts or determines anything in the real world. The divisions of the day are genuine, verifiable arithmetic on measured sunrise and sunset times; the qualities assigned to them have no demonstrated connection to events. Treat every grading as a faithful reconstruction of how the pañcāṅga tradition reasoned — interesting history, not guidance. See Sources & Science.
