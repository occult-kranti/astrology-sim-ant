The Atharvan roots — the oldest layer

Long before the tantric ṣaṭkarman systematized the six acts, the Atharvaveda (the Śaunaka Saṃhitā, c. 1200-1000 BCE) already held a large corpus of abhicāra hymns — charms against enemies, sorcerers and demons — together with the kṛtyā counter-magic that turns a spell back on its maker. The Kauśika Sūtra is the manual that tells you which hymn belongs to which rite. This is the stratum the later tantras inherited and reorganized.

Described as history — the frame holds here too These hymns are quoted only in their public-domain scholarly translations (Bloomfield 1897; Whitney-Lanman 1905), by short title and purpose, exactly as an Indology reference would list them. The Kauśika Sūtra numbers say which hymn a rite used; they are bibliographic pointers, not a set of instructions. Nothing here has any demonstrated validity, and nothing here tells anyone how to do anything.

The Vedic → tantric continuity

The through-line is direct. The Atharvan sorcerer's toolkit — hymns to destroy an enemy, to eject a rival, to make a man impotent, to expose and burn hidden wizards — is the same conceptual space the medieval tantras would later carve into vaśīkaraṇa, uccāṭana, māraṇa and the rest, with their seasons and colours and gestures. And the Vedic layer already carried an expiation: Atharvaveda 16.2, recited while washing the whole body, "for removing all sins committed during the performance of the ritual" (Kauśika Sūtra 49.27, the closing sūtra of the witchcraft section). The defensive half of the corpus — the kṛtyāpratiharaṇa ("repelling of witchcraft") and cātana ("expelling") charms — is just as old as the aggressive half.

What a kṛtyā is

A kṛtyā is the sorcery itself made into a thing — often a fashioned figure — that is "prepared" and sent against a victim; Whitney glosses the opening of AV 10.1 as "She whom the adepts prepare, all-formed, hand-made." The counter-charms above answer it directly: they name the kṛtyā, reverse its direction, and return it to its sender. This pratyabhicāra ("counter-sorcery") is the Vedic ancestor of everything the later tantras would call defensive ritual, and it is the reason the tradition could hold both an aggressive and a protective self-image at once.
