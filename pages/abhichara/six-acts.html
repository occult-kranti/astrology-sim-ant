The six acts, one by one

The tantric texts systematized ritual magic into the six acts — the ṣaṭkarman — and then hung an elaborate lattice of correspondences on them: each act has its season, its hour, its direction, its deity, its colour, its posture-mat, its gesture, its rosary and its finger-hold. This page lays out the verified tables in full, act by act, shows the places where the texts contradict themselves (flagged ⚑, never resolved), and lets you cast the timing screen for any moment.

Described, never prescribed Every table below is catalogued data, presented as the scholarship presents it — the same colour, season and gesture lists that Princeton UP and Motilal print. None of it has any demonstrated validity, and none of it is an instruction. The māraṇa ("slaying") section carries only the symbolic and classificatory correspondences and states what the texts CLAIM, as religious history — there is no procedure here that could function as a rite.

Always "six" — even when it isn't

The group is called ṣaṭkarmāṇi ("six acts") almost universally, but the actual lists differ text to text — a text's index verse will promise six while its body treats seven or eight. "Index verses differing from the actual contents is common, if not universal, in the magic tantras" (Ullrey). The site keeps every variant; it never silently picks one.

The hand, and the fortnight

The left-hand rule ⚑

All the acts use the right hand except vaśya (subjugation), which uses the left. ⚑ The rule as stated depends on the commentary's inverted gloss: the root verse says the other acts use the savya hand (savya normally = left), but Bandhuṣeṇa's commentary glosses savya as dakṣiṇa ("right") and assigns vaśya to the vāma (left) hand.
BPK 3.7 + comm. (Ullrey p. 161 nn. 36-37).

The dark fortnight

māraṇa (with stambhana) belongs to the dark (kṛṣṇa) half of the lunar month — "a rite such as liquidation should be mainly connected with the dark half" (Bühnemann): a dark rite during dark nights, on the 8th/14th of the dark fortnight and the new moon. The benign śānti / pauṣṭika rites take the bright (śukla) half; cow-hide mats are prescribed for appeasement.
MMU 25, Tables 26.3-26.4 (Bühnemann p. 449); Ullrey p. 135.

The six acts, one by one

1 · śānti / śāntika — pacification

benign

The one wholly benign act. Bühnemann renders the Mantramahodadhi's definition as "the destruction of diseases" — the appeasement of illness, planetary evil and hostile sorcery — and it carries the two positive sub-aims rakṣā (protection) and puṣṭi (prosperity, increase). White colour, the lotus gesture, coral or diamond beads, the bright fortnight; in scheme A its season is hemanta (winter), but the BPK puts it at midnight and U 1.28 at day's end. MMU 25.1-3 (Bühnemann p. 454).

2 · vaśīkaraṇa / vaśya — subjugation

coercive

Bringing a person, animal or deity under one's will. Bühnemann's gloss of the MMU definition is stark — "the state of one who executes a command… especially seducing a woman against her will" — the erotic-domination sense is explicit in the text. It is the act singled out as performed with the left hand. Red colour, the noose (pāśa) gesture, coral/diamond beads; season vasanta (spring), day-part forenoon. MMU 25.1-3; U 1.75; BPK 3.7.

3 · stambhana — immobilization

hostile

"The obstructing of an activity" (Bühnemann). To paralyse or arrest — armies, weapons, speech, water, bodily functions, a rival's mind. Yellow (turmeric) colour, the club (gadā) gesture; season śiśira (cool), grouped with māraṇa as suited to the dark fortnight. Its mat is "no mark" (vikaṭa) in the Uḍḍīśatantra, the axe/thunderbolt (kuliśa) in the BPK. MMU 25.1-3; U 1.71-72; BPK 3.9.

4 · vidveṣaṇa / vidveṣa — causing discord

hostile

"Dislike between two affectionate people" (Bühnemann) — breaking alliances and sowing enmity between allies, friends or spouses (prīti-nāśana, "destruction of affection"). Smoke-grey colour, the pestle (musala) gesture, a horse-tooth rosary strung on a thread of the victim's hair, the thumb-and-index hold; season grīṣma (summer), day-part midday. MMU 25.1-3; U 1.103-113.

5 · uccāṭana — eradication, driving-away

hostile

"Deprivation from a place" (Bühnemann) — to force a target out of home, family, office or kingdom, to make them wander. Smoke-grey colour, the arrowhead/vajra gesture, the same horse-tooth rosary as vidveṣa; season varṣā / prāvṛṭ (the rains), day-part afternoon. Its Uḍḍīśatantra mat is the half-svastika. MMU 25.1-3; U 1.71-113.

6 · māraṇa — slaying ("liquidation")

māraṇa-class
The strongest frame — read this This entry records only what the texts CLAIM, and their symbolic correspondences, presented strictly as religious history. There is no procedure here: no method, no materials-of-harm, nothing that could function as an instruction. Bühnemann's MMU gloss is "the taking of life"; Goudriaan and Bühnemann render the term "liquidation," while Ullrey keeps "murder" — a live scholarly dispute ⚑.

Its correspondences, as catalogued: black (asita) colour, the sword (khaḍga) gesture, the thumb-and-little-finger hold; the "no mark" (vikaṭa) mat in the Uḍḍīśatantra. Its timing is the one the three schemes fight over most — autumn and the dark fortnight (scheme A, U 1.17), versus twilight (U 1.28) — and Bühnemann notes it "should be mainly connected with the dark half" of the lunar month: "a dark rite during dark nights." MMU 25.1-3 (Bühnemann p. 454); U 1.75, 1.103-113; Bühnemann p. 449.

How this works

The calendar season (ṛtu)

The Indian ṛtu spans two solar months (two rāśis). The screen takes the Sun's sidereal longitude (Lahiri ayanāṁśa, via the site's Vedic engine), reads its rāśi, and maps it to the season. This site uses the common pañcāṅga solar-ṛtu pairing that begins Vasanta at Mīna–Meṣa (Pisces–Aries): Vasanta = Mīna+Meṣa, Grīṣma = Vṛṣabha+Mithuna, Varṣā = Karka+Siṃha, Śarad = Kanyā+Tulā, Hemanta = Vṛścika+Dhanu, Śiśira = Makara+Kumbha. ⚑ A second common convention starts Vasanta one sign later (Meṣa–Vṛṣabha …); the two disagree at every rāśi boundary, and the choice is flagged, not hidden. Then Table C (Uḍḍīśatantra 1.16-17 / MMU 25) maps each season to an act: hemanta→śānti, vasanta→vaśya, śiśira→stambhana, grīṣma→vidveṣa, varṣā→uccāṭana, śarad→māraṇa. (cf. Sūrya-Siddhānta 14.10; drikpanchang solar-ṛtu.)

The ghaṭikā cycle (scheme A, within the day)

From sunrise — taken from the same rise/set search the planetary-hours engine uses, never re-derived — the day-night cycle is divided into blocks of ten ghaṭikās (a ghaṭikā is a fixed 24-minute unit, so ten make four hours). The six four-hour blocks cycle the six seasons spring→summer→rains→fall→cold→cool; the block you are in points, by Table C, to its act. (Correction applied: ten ghaṭikās per season-block — not "six ghaṭikā-units".)

The day-part (scheme B) and the fortnight

The day-part follows the Bhairavapadmāvatīkalpa 3.6-7 six-part scheme (pūrvāhṇa · madhyāhna · parāhṇa by day; sandhyā · ardharātra · prabhāta by night). The screen locates the moment by equal thirds of the measured day arc (sunrise→sunset) and night arc (sunset→next sunrise) — a modelling convention, since the texts name the six parts but not their exact clock bounds. The lunar fortnight (śukla / kṛṣṇa) comes straight from the pañcāṅga tithi. Where the day-part and season schemes point different ways — and they do — the screen reports the disagreement rather than resolving it.

What "match" means here

"Matches / differs" is purely a calendar coincidence check: does the current season, block, day-part or fortnight equal the one the tradition prescribed for the chosen act? A "match" is a statement about the calendar, nothing more — it carries no claim that anything would happen, because nothing does. Every row cites its source verse.

A strong, honest caveat None of these timing correspondences predicts or determines anything in the real world. The divisions of the day and year are genuine, verifiable arithmetic; the acts, seasons and gestures assigned to them are a faithful reconstruction of how the abhicāra tradition reasoned — interesting religious history, not guidance, and never an instruction.
