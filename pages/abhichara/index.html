Abhicāra & the ṣaṭkarman — the Indian ritual-magic traditions, described

This wing studies the Indian traditions of ritual magic — the Vedic abhicāra of the Atharvaveda and its systematization in the tantric ṣaṭkarman, "the six acts": śānti (pacification), vaśīkaraṇa (subjugation), stambhana (immobilization), vidveṣaṇa (sowing discord), uccāṭana (driving away) and māraṇa (slaying). It presents them exactly as the academic literature does — as religious history and belief-system analysis — and nothing more.

Please read this first — the honest frame

These are historical, religious symbolic systems with no demonstrated validity. Everything here is described, never prescribed. "Abhicāra" is the tradition's own word for hostile ritual; "black magic" is a colonial-era and modern-popular gloss, not the texts' own category, and this wing keeps the scholarly frame instead. The māraṇa-class ("slaying") material states only what the texts CLAIM, as history — there are no dosages, no step-by-step rites, and no "how to harm" anywhere in this wing.

What you will find are the same catalogued correspondence tables that Princeton University Press and Motilal Banarsidass themselves print — colours, seasons, gestures, rosary materials — plus the calendar arithmetic of when the tradition said each act "belonged". This page exists to explain a historical belief-system; reading it, or computing the timing screen, does nothing in the world.

Why this belongs on the Workbench

The rest of this site computes the astrology of Lilly and the Picatrix as history made calculable, and it already carries the Latin magical corpus — the planetary talismans, the decan images, the kameas — as catalogued data with citations, never as working spells. The Indian ritual-magic tradition is the eastern mirror of exactly that material. Where the Picatrix assigns a suffumigation and an hour to each planet, the Mantramahodadhi assigns a season, a direction, a colour and a gesture to each of the six acts; where the West has its grimoires, the Sanskrit world has the Uḍḍīśatantra and the Bhairavapadmāvatīkalpa. Presenting the two on the same honest terms — real philology, no claimed efficacy — is the whole point.

The taxonomy at a glance

The tantric texts almost always call the group "the six" (ṣaṭkarman), even when a given text actually lists seven or eight — a quirk the six-acts page lays out in full. In Mahīdhara's standard order the six run from the one benign act to the gravest:

The ethics the tradition itself applied

The sources are not silent on restraint, and the honest thing is to show both sides. On one hand, the six acts are classed as kāmya rites — optional, desire-driven — in the nitya / naimittika / kāmya schema, so that (in the tradition's own logic) only someone already established in daily and occasional worship was held eligible to attempt them at all. Mahīdhara wraps his chapter in an expiation layer: he says he described the desire-oriented rites "only for the sake of persons who are attracted to sense pleasures," and that feeding brahmans after the fire-ritual "destroy[s] the sin that accrues from the performance of destructive magic." The oldest, Vedic, layer already had a post-abhicāra purification (Atharvaveda 16.2).

On the other hand — and this is Ullrey's necessary caveat — the restraint is real but partial. The Uḍḍīśa grimoire body rarely regulates itself outside the later commentaries, and some rites state their motive nakedly: the desire to usurp a victim's wife, children and wealth. We record the restraints where the texts place them, and we do not pretend they were more than they were.

Sources: Gupta, in Hindu Tantrism (Brill 1979) pp. 124-126, via Ullrey, Grim Grimoires (UCSB diss. 2016) pp. 139-140; Bühnemann pp. 452-453; Ullrey p. 135 n. 53.
