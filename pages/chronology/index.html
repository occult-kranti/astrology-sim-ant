The maxim above, paraphrased from the Emerald Tablet, became the signature of a tradition that crossed languages and empires — from the star-priests of Babylon and the scribes of Egypt, through Roman Alexandria and the Arabic translators of Harran, to Ficino's Florence, John Dee's angelic tables, and the magical orders of the modern age. Honest note — please read This wing is the history of ideas, not an endorsement: Hermetic philosophy, alchemy and ritual magic are presented as historical symbolic systems with no demonstrated validity — described, never prescribed. Its promise is the epistemic label on every entry: documented history is kept visibly distinct from disputed readings, debunked claims, and conspiracy narratives that have no evidentiary basis. The four labels

documented
Established by primary texts or peer-reviewed scholarship.
disputed
Contested among scholars, or popular but not settled.
debunked
A specific claim disproven by verifiable evidence.
conspiracy
No evidentiary basis; shown as discourse, not fact.
Method note This is a curated overview, not an exhaustive register, and dates for ancient and medieval texts are scholarly estimates rather than fixed points. The labels exist to keep a real distinction visible: the Hermetic tradition is a genuine, well-studied strand of intellectual history, while the modern “Illuminati bloodline” and “New World Order” narratives that borrow its imagery have no evidentiary basis and descend from a documented chain of fabrication — not from the sources catalogued here. Where history is uncertain, it is marked disputed; where a specific claim has been disproven, debunked.

Why this belongs on this site

The Workbench computes the astrology of Lilly and the Picatrix as history made calculable; this wing supplies that history's spine. The Picatrix sits at the exact centre of this timeline — compiled in al-Andalus from the Sabian and Hermetic sources above it, translated for Alfonso X, and feeding the Renaissance magic of Ficino, Agrippa and Dee below it. The chronology shows where each book, ritual and doctrine on this site came from, who carried it, and — crucially — which parts of the popular story are documented, which are disputed, and which are fabrications. Same rule as everywhere on the site: the dates and transmissions are real scholarship; the magic's claims have no demonstrated validity. See Sources & Science.
