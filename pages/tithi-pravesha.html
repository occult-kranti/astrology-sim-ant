Tithi-praveśa — the Vedic annual tithi return

Where the Western solar revolution restarts the year when the Sun regains its natal place, the tithi-praveśa restarts it when the Moon regains its exact natal angle from the Sun — the same tithi (lunar day) and the same tithi-fraction — while the Sun is back in its natal sidereal sign. It is Sanjay Rath's Odiśā-tradition annual chart (srath.com); P.V.R. Narasimha Rao refined it to match the soli-lunar month instead, and Jagannath Hora offers both with solar months as the shipped default — the default here. The chart is cast for the birthplace, and read for the TP lagna, the weekday lord of the day, and the horā lord of the instant (the "lord of the year").

Honest note — please read Unlike almost everything else on this site, tithi-praveśa has no classical BPHS or Jaimini textual anchor: it is a modern lineage teaching (Rath attributes it to his Odiśā tradition; PVR Narasimha Rao refined it). The astronomy is exact and verifiable — the elongation match is ayanāṁśa-independent — but the technique's authority rests on a living school, not a cited verse, and the solar-vs-soli-lunar month choice is a genuine dispute (Rao himself cautions neither convention always wins). Rath's school also prefers a non-Lahiri ayanāṁśa (Puṣya-pakṣa/SSS); this engine stays on Lahiri, and only the month-window edges depend on that. Every one of these forks is shown, never silently resolved. It has no demonstrated validity — described, never prescribed. See Sources & Science.

How this works — the tithi-praveśa, step by step

The elongation, not the Sun

Every tithi is 12° of the Moon-minus-Sun elongation; the natal tithi-fraction is the exact angle E₀ = (Moon − Sun) at birth. The annual return is the instant that angle recurs. Because it is a difference of two longitudes, the ayanāṁśa cancels — the elongation match is ayanāṁśa-independent, identical whether you reckon tropically or sidereally. The Moon gains on the Sun at 11.8–15.4°/day against the Sun's ~1°/day, so the elongation is strictly increasing and crosses E₀ upward about once per synodic month.

The month window (the one ayanāṁśa-dependent step)

Rath's rule (the JHora default) qualifies the return: it must fall while the Sun occupies its natal sidereal sign. So the engine finds the Sun's sidereal ingress into that sign in the target year and its egress (a 29.4–31.5-day window), then scans the window hour by hour for upward crossings of E₀ and bisects each to under a second — exactly the bracket-and-bisect pattern of the site's solar-return.js/returns.js. Only this window depends on the ayanāṁśa (Lahiri here, flagged); the crossing itself does not.

Zero or two crossings — surfaced, never guessed

A sidereal solar month (≈29.4–31.5 d) is not the synodic month (29.27–29.83 d), so a window can hold two matches (a long month) or none (a short one — Sagittarius/Capricorn). The engine returns every candidate in the window with an ambiguity flag rather than silently pick one; where a match is skipped it surfaces the nearest, flagged. The soli-lunar variant (PVR) instead matches the return in the same amānta month as birth — which can select a different instant (the classic demonstration case: a September match that the solar rule excludes because the Sun has not yet entered the natal sign).

What is read from the chart

The chart is cast for the birthplace and judged sidereally on the site's Vedic engine (castVedic): the TP lagna is read like a natal lagna for the year; the weekday lord of the TP day and the horā lord at the TP instant (the "lord of the year", whose houses in the rāśi and vargas gain weight — the horā lord reuses the Chaldean planetary-hour engine). The tradition pairs the chart with a compressed Tithi-Aṣṭottarī daśā; that is a documented reading-layer convention (Rath/PVR), shown but not computed — it has no BPHS anchor and its apportionment varies by lineage.
