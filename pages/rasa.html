Rasaśāstra & yantra-mathematics — the mercury alchemy and the magic squares of India

Two Indian threads meet here. Rasaśāstra is the medieval alchemy of mercury (pārada): a dated chain of tantric texts that claimed mercury, refined through eighteen saṃskāras in a workshop of named yantras (apparatus), could turn base metal to gold (lohavedha) and perfect the body toward immortality (dehavedha). It did neither — but its mercury–sulphur chemistry is a real chapter in the history of chemistry. The second thread is yantra-mathematics: the nine navagraha magic squares, the dated classical squares (Varāhamihira's, the Khajuraho Chautisa), the kaṭapayādi letter-numbers, and the 81-cell Sarvatobhadra Chakra — provable arithmetic, its provenance flagged honestly.

Honest note — please read These are historical symbolic systems of no demonstrated validity — described here for study, never prescribed. The mathematics is real: every magic square on this page is verified live in your browser and proven again in the engine's test suite. The readings — planetary yantras, the Chakra's vedha — are the tradition's, reproduced with their sources and never a claim about the world. Provenance is flagged: the nine navagraha squares are the modern printed ("bazaar") tradition, kept separate from the dated classical squares; the Sarvatobhadra second ring rests on a single degraded source and is flagged ⚑ accordingly. The Nāgārjuna authorship, the text datings, and the Chakra's rules are contested — both positions kept, never resolved.
⚠ Toxicity — read before anything else The substances named below — mercury and its compounds (cinnabar/HgS, mercury chloride), lead, arsenic, orpiment and realgar, and their calcined "bhasmas" — are poisons. Mercury compounds are neurotoxic and nephrotoxic; there is no safe home preparation. Everything here is the historical record of what the texts describe, presented as history of chemistry only. It is not instructions: no dosages, no "how to prepare", no medical use is given or implied. Do not attempt any of it.

The nine navagraha yantras (verified live — modern printed tradition)

Each planet's 3×3 square is L1 + (k−1): the same base square shifted up by one in every cell, nine times over. Magic constants run 15, 18, 21 … 39 (step 3), the nine totals 45 … 117 (step 9), the grand total 729 = 9³. The verifier badge below each grid runs validateYantra() in your browser right now. Provenance: this exact offset set is the modern printed yantra tradition (Styan 2012 found no other depicted) — not a dated classical text; contrast Agrippa's kameas, where Saturn (not the Sun) takes the 3×3.

The dated classical squares (kept separate from the bazaar set)

These carry real dates and citations. The Chautisa of Khajuraho is a most-perfect pandiagonal square — not just rows, columns and diagonals, but every broken diagonal, every wrapping 2×2 block and the corner quads all sum to 34. Each property is checked live below.

Kaṭapayādi — the letter-numbers

The Kerala system that hides numbers in words: consonants map to digits, only the last consonant of a cluster counts, vowelless consonants are dropped, and the digits are read right-to-left. Earliest attested use: Haridatta, 683 CE — the historical bridge to the KP / vākya material. Decode a word, or encode a number.

The Sarvatobhadra Chakra (cast for a chosen date — joins the pañcāṅga)

The "auspicious-in-every-direction" transit grid: 81 cells in five nested squares — 28 nakṣatras on the outer border, 16 vowels on the diagonals, 20 consonants (ring 2, flagged ⚑ — single degraded source), 12 rāśis, and the tithi-group/weekday cells. Cast a moment and the grid highlights the Moon's nakṣatra (and every graha's), the current tithi-group cell and the weekday cell, then lists the Moon's vedha (piercing) lines.

How this works

The magic squares are provable — the readings are not

Every grid here is checked live by validateYantra() in core/yantra.js: it sums each row, each column and both diagonals and confirms they equal one magic constant. (Unlike Agrippa's kameas it does not demand the cells be a permutation of 1…n² — the navagraha grids use consecutive integers, and Varāhamihira's square repeats 1…8.) The Chautisa gets the stronger validateMostPerfect(), which also checks all broken diagonals, all sixteen wrapping 2×2 blocks and the corner quads. The engine's headless test proves all of it on every load, so a corrupt grid can never ship. What the arithmetic cannot do is make a planetary talisman work — the yantra "meanings" are historical symbolism, of no demonstrated effect.

The navagraha set is modern; the classical anchors are older

The nine planet-squares are one family — Lk = L1 + (k−1) over the double-flipped Lo Shu — and are the modern printed ("bazaar") tradition: Styan's 2012 survey found no other set depicted, and no dated classical text carries them (the bazaar is not even uniform — some vendors sell the Western Agrippa kameas as graha yantras instead). The genuinely dated squares are kept separate: Varāhamihira's perfume-square of c. 550 CE (Bṛhatsaṃhitā ch. 77 — ch. 76 in Hayashi's numbering), the Khajuraho Chautisa (temple c. 950–970 CE, inscription dated 12th c. by some — the honest range is 10th–12th c.), the Nāgārjunīya construction (sum 100), and the Gargasaṃhitā's 3×3-for-navagraha idea (passage not before 400 CE).

Kaṭapayādi decoding

The decoder tokenises an IAST string, groups consonant clusters, and applies the rules: the last consonant of a cluster takes its digit when a vowel follows; a bare vowel counts 0; a trailing vowelless consonant is ignored; then the digit-string is read right-to-left. It reproduces the verified vectors — Dhīraśaṅkarābharaṇam → 29, Mechakalyāṇī → 65 (both use only the two-syllable melakartā prefix), and the famous π verse bhadrāmbudhi… → the 18 digits 314159265358979324 (= π × 10¹⁷ rounded). Encoding is one-to-many (each digit has several consonants), so the encoder returns one representative spelling that round-trips.

The Sarvatobhadra Chakra & its vedha

buildSarvatobhadra() places all 81 cells from the construction rules: the 28 nakṣatras clockwise from the NE (Īśāna) corner (with Abhijit inserted between Uttarāṣāḍhā and Śravaṇa), the 16 vowels on the two diagonals, the 20 consonants of the flagged second ring, the 12 rāśis, the tithi-group and weekday cells, and Pūrṇā + Saturday at the centre. Casting a moment reuses the site's own pañcāṅga (castChart → castVedic) to find where the Moon and the grahas sit. The vedha (piercing) lookup returns the three classical lines from a border nakṣatra — the straight "front" cell and the two diagonals — but which diagonal acts (forward vs hind) depends on the planet's motion, and both the luminaries/nodes rule and the Chakra's primary source (Narapatijayacaryā 1176 CE vs the Brahmayāmala) are contested and kept unresolved in the data.

A strong, honest caveat Refining mercury never made gold or granted immortality, and inscribing a planet's square on a plate does nothing to the world. The chemistry was real and often toxic; the alchemical and astrological claims have no demonstrated validity. What remains is worth studying as history — of chemistry (Ray), of religion (White), and of a mathematics of magic squares that genuinely predates much of the world's. See Sources & Science.
The Latin sibling The West built its own planet-squares — Agrippa's seven kameas, from Saturn's 3×3 to the Moon's 9×9, traced with spirit-sigils. India's navagraha set is the same idea with the opposite planetary logic (Sūrya, not Saturn, takes the 3×3). Two traditions, one mathematics — compared, never merged.
