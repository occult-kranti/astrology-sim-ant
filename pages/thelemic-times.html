How this works

The Era Legis notation — counted from the 1904 equinox

The Thelemic calendar counts years from the northern vernal equinox of 1904 (the New Aeon epoch; the year Liber AL vel Legis was received). Years are gathered into 22-year cycles Crowley called docosades. A date is written as two Roman numerals — the docosade (upper-case) and the year within it 0–21 (lower-case) — so the letter-case alone distinguishes them (e.g. Vxii is docosade 5, year 12). The epoch year 1904 is year 0 and renders 00 (there is no Roman zero). The full stamp adds the Sun's and Moon's zodiacal longitudes at the moment (☉ in … : ☽ in …, computed on the same engine every chart on this site uses) and the Latin day-name (dies Solis … dies Saturni). This is reproduced as Crowley's calendrical practice, cited to his diaries and The Equinox — a notation, not an astrological claim.

The contested year-start — shown both ways

When exactly does the Thelemic year turn? Crowley's own dating followed the true vernal-equinox instant (a moment that drifts a little year to year); a fixed March 20 boundary is a later simplification some modern Thelemites use. The two agree except for the few hours between March 20 00:00 and the true equinox — and for a moment in that window the tool shows both answers and resolves neither (the default is the exact-equinox convention). This is the same both-ways discipline the rest of the site applies to every contested rule.

The Liber Resh stations — astronomy the text names

Liber Resh vel Helios (1911) has the aspirant salute the Sun at four times of the day — dawn, noon, sunset and midnight — under the classical names Ra, Ahathoor, Tum and Khephra (the sun rising, at its height, setting, and beneath the earth). What this page computes is only the astronomy those names mark: sunrise and sunset (the Sun crossing the horizon — reused from the site's planetary-hours engine), solar noon (the Sun's upper culmination on the meridian) and solar midnight (its lower culmination, beneath the earth). The adoration names are recorded as a cited historical datum from the public-domain text; the page describes that the tradition performs these adorations, never that anyone should. Every station is honestly labelled with the astronomy it is.
